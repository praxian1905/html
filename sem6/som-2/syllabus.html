Unit 02

02. Theories of Elastic Failure

🔷 Unit Overview

Studies the classical failure theories with full derivations and graphical representations, then applies them to realistic combined loading scenarios in both 2-D and 3-D stress states.

Failure Theories

  • Maximum Normal Stress Theory (Rankine)
  • Maximum Shear Stress Theory (Tresca)
  • Maximum Strain Energy Theory (Haigh)
  • Maximum Distortion (shear strain) Energy Theory (von Mises)
  • Maximum Normal (or Principal) Strain Theory (St. Venant)
  • Derivations and graphical representations for each theory

Applications — Combined Loading

  • 2-D & 3-D stress systems
  • (i) Combined direct loading and bending
  • (ii) Combined torsional and direct loading
THEORY 01

Tresca Criterion

Failure when max shear stress reaches the yield value in simple tension: τ_max = σ_y / 2.

THEORY 02 ★

von Mises Criterion

Failure when distortion strain energy equals that at yielding. Most widely used in engineering practice.

THEORY 03

Rankine Criterion

Failure when the largest principal stress equals tensile strength. Applicable to brittle materials.

  • U = P² L / (2AE) — linear elastic case

  • Energy method using equating kinetic and strain energies

Energy in Structural Members

  • Strain energy of beams in bending; beam deflections via Castigliano's theorem
  • Strain energy of shafts in twisting

Castigliano's 2nd Theorem: δᵢ = ∂U / ∂Pᵢ where U = total strain energy Pᵢ = applied force at point i δᵢ = deflection at Pᵢ in its direction

Unit 06

06. Bending of Curved Bars

🔷 Unit Overview

Extends beam bending theory to members with large initial curvature where Euler–Bernoulli assumptions break down. Covers neutral axis shift, stress distribution, and practical applications to crane hooks and circular rings.

Theory of Curved Beams

  • Bending of beams with large initial curvature — Winkler–Bach theory
  • Position of neutral axis for rectangular cross-section
  • Position of neutral axis for trapezoidal cross-section
  • Position of neutral axis for circular cross-section

Applications

  • Stresses in crane hooks — trapezoidal and circular sections
  • Stresses in circular rings subjected to tension
  • Stresses in circular rings subjected to compression
Winkler–Bach Formula for curved beams: σ = M (y) / [A e (R_n + y)] where M = bending moment A = cross-sectional area e = R_c - R_n (eccentricity) R_c = radius to centroidal axis R_n = radius to neutral axis y = distance from neutral axis
In curved beams the neutral axis shifts towards the centre of curvature, unlike in straight beams where it passes through the centroid. This shift is the origin of the characteristic hyperbolic stress distribution.
