Chapter 1

01. Introduction — Understanding the Problem

1.1 Why Electric Vehicles?

Electric Vehicles (EVs) have emerged as the most promising alternative to petrol/diesel cars. The primary drivers are: rising environmental pollution from fossil fuel combustion, the depletion of fossil fuel reserves, and global demand for sustainable transportation. EVs run on rechargeable lithium-ion battery packs, offering high efficiency, near-zero emissions, quieter operation, and lower running costs. EV adoption is growing rapidly across both personal and commercial vehicle segments.

1.2 Why Does Battery Thermal Management Matter?

Lithium-ion batteries are extremely sensitive to temperature. During charging and discharging cycles, batteries generate heat due to two main reasons:

REASON 01

Electrochemical Reactions

The internal chemical reactions that store/release energy themselves produce heat as a byproduct.

REASON 02

Internal Resistance

Electrical current flowing through the battery's internal resistance (Joule heating) generates heat proportional to I²R.

If this heat is not properly managed, temperature rises beyond the safe operating range (≈20°C–45°C), affecting battery life and performance. A Battery Thermal Management System (BTMS) is therefore essential. Its job: maintain uniform temperature distribution across all battery cells.

1.3 Battery Overheating — What Goes Wrong?

⚠ Consequences of Battery Overheating

Excessive temperature rise causes a cascade of problems — some gradual, some catastrophic:

EFFECT A

Reduced Efficiency

Charging efficiency drops; more energy wasted as heat.

EFFECT B

Capacity Degradation

Faster degradation of battery capacity — battery holds less charge over time.

EFFECT C

Increased Resistance

Higher internal resistance further worsens heat generation — a vicious cycle.

EFFECT D

Thermal Runaway

The worst case: uncontrolled temperature escalation leading to fire or explosion.

Additionally, non-uniform temperature distribution across cells (some hotter than others) reduces overall reliability and lifespan of the battery pack.

1.4 What are Phase Change Materials (PCM)?

A Phase Change Material is a substance that absorbs and stores a large amount of heat when it transitions from one phase to another — typically from solid → liquid (melting).

🔑 Key Principle: Latent Heat

When a PCM melts, it absorbs a large quantity of heat called latent heat while maintaining nearly constant temperature. This temperature plateau is ideal for battery cooling — the PCM acts as a thermal buffer, absorbing heat spikes without letting the temperature spike.

PCM-based passive cooling systems offer: compact design, silent operation, no moving parts or external power, and effective temperature regulation.

1.5 Why Paraffin Wax Specifically? Why RT42?

Among all PCMs, paraffin wax is the most popular for low-to-medium temperature applications because:

High latent heat storage Chemically stable Non-corrosive Non-toxic Cost-effective Reliable thermal cycling

RT42 is a specific grade of paraffin wax with a melting point of approximately 42°C, which is right at the upper safe limit for lithium-ion batteries. This means RT42 begins absorbing heat precisely when the battery is about to overheat — perfect timing. However, there is one major problem:

❌ Critical Limitation

Pure paraffin wax has a very low thermal conductivity of ~0.2 W/m·K. This means heat travels through it very slowly — think of it as a thermal insulator rather than a heat spreader. For fast-responding battery cooling, this is a serious bottleneck.

1.6 The Solution: Conductive Additives

The solution is to add highly thermally conductive particles into the paraffin wax. These particles create a network of heat-conducting pathways inside the PCM, enabling rapid heat transfer. Three additives are explored in this project:

ADDITIVE 01

Aluminium Powder

k ≈ 205–237 W/m·K. Affordable and widely available. Improves bulk heat transfer.

ADDITIVE 02

Graphite Powder

k ≈ 100–400 W/m·K. Excellent layered structure creates 2D heat pathways. Very cost-effective.

ADDITIVE 03

Carbon Nanotubes (CNT)

k ≈ 2000–6000 W/m·K. Extraordinary conductivity at very low concentrations (<1 wt%).

1.7 Project Objectives (Summary)

✅ What This Project Aims to Do

1. Study RT42 paraffin wax as a PCM for EV cooling.
2. Prepare doped composite PCM samples (Al, Graphite, CNT).
3. Design and build an experimental apparatus.
4. Measure thermal conductivity, specific heat capacity, melting behaviour, temperature distribution, and volumetric expansion.
5. Compare all composites and identify the optimum composition.

03. Literature Review — What Previous Researchers Found

The literature review surveys existing published research to justify the current study and avoid reinventing the wheel. Here is what the review found:

The Core Problem (Established by Prior Research)

📚 What Literature Confirmed

PCM-based passive cooling is effective for EV batteries. RT42 paraffin wax works well because of its ~42°C melting point and high latent heat. But its very low thermal conductivity (~0.2 W/m·K) is consistently identified as the critical limitation across all literature.

What Additives Researchers Have Tried

Additive Typical Concentration What It Does Drawback
Aluminium Powder 3–10 wt% Improves bulk heat transfer, reduces melting time, improves temperature uniformity Lower conductivity than CNT/graphite; adds weight
Graphite Powder 5–10 wt% Excellent heat distribution, thermal stability, reduces thermal resistance High concentrations increase viscosity, reduce latent heat
Carbon Nanotubes 0.5–1 wt% Rapid heat transfer even at tiny concentrations; thermal conductivity up to 6000 W/m·K Expensive; prone to agglomeration; difficult to disperse uniformly
Biochar (literature) Varied Porous carbon structure improves stability and leakage resistance Less effective than CNT alone
Graphene Nanoplatelets Varied Significantly reduced battery surface temperature; improved uniformity Very expensive; complex to produce

Notable Study: Balasundaram & Sivaram (2024)

This study investigated paraffin + biochar (from Prosopis Juliflora plant) + aluminium powder. Using FT-IR, XRD, and SEM characterization, they found that the porous carbon structure improved structural stability while aluminium enhanced thermal conductance. Under a heat flux of 2.3 kW/m², the composite PCM reduced base temperature by 8.2°C and improved thermal conductance by ~50% compared to pure paraffin.

Industrial Application Study: Motor Cooling

Paraffin PCM was placed inside an Al6061 aluminium alloy casing around a 75 W AC motor. DS18B20 sensors and an ESP32 IoT system monitored temperature. Result: peak motor temperature dropped from 92.5°C to 64.5°C — a 28°C reduction. This directly validates the approach used in this project.

Research Gap Identified (Why THIS Study is Needed)

🔍 The Gap in Existing Literature

Most studies investigate aluminium, graphite, or CNT individually. Very few study their combined effect in one PCM system. Also, most studies don't simultaneously evaluate thermal conductivity, specific heat capacity, AND volumetric expansion under identical conditions. This project fills that gap.

04. Materials & Their Thermophysical Properties

4.1 RT42 Paraffin Wax — Why This Specific Grade?

RT42 is an organic, hydrocarbon-based PCM composed mainly of long-chain linear alkane molecules (like C₂₄H₅₀). The "RT" stands for Rubitherm, the name of the manufacturer (Rubitherm Technologies GmbH) and "42" refers to its melting point in °C.

Lithium-ion batteries operate efficiently between 20°C and 45°C. When temperature exceeds ~45°C, degradation accelerates rapidly. RT42's melting point of ~42°C means it starts absorbing heat right before the battery crosses its danger threshold. It acts like a thermal fuse — absorbing heat spikes and preventing temperature runaway.

Property Typical Value What It Means
Melting Temperature 40–44°C Starts melting right at battery's danger zone — perfectly timed absorption
Latent Heat of Fusion 170–220 kJ/kg Can absorb 170–220 kilojoules per kilogram during melting — huge heat reservoir
Density (Solid) 880–900 kg/m³ Denser when solid than liquid — expands on melting
Density (Liquid) 760–780 kg/m³ Less dense when liquid — explains the 10–15% volumetric expansion
Thermal Conductivity 0.2–0.25 W/m·K Very poor — the critical limitation this project addresses
Specific Heat Capacity 2–2.5 kJ/kg·K Good heat storage even before melting
Volumetric Expansion 10–15% Expands significantly on melting — container must allow for this
Flash Point >150°C Won't catch fire until 150°C — safe for EV applications

4.2 Aluminium Powder — The Economical Enhancer

Aluminium powder is added to create metal particle conduction bridges throughout the wax. Heat can travel along these metallic pathways much faster than through the wax alone.

Property Value Relevance
Thermal Conductivity 205–237 W/m·K ~1000× better than pure paraffin — every particle is a heat highway
Density 2700 kg/m³ 3× denser than paraffin — adds weight but manageable
Specific Heat Capacity 0.9 kJ/kg·K Much lower than paraffin — dilutes the composite's heat storage
Melting Point 660°C Won't melt anywhere near PCM temperatures — structurally stable
Corrosion Resistance Good Forms stable oxide layer — safe for repeated use
⚖ Trade-off

Aluminium particles don't undergo phase change themselves, so every gram of aluminium displaces a gram of wax that would have stored latent heat. Therefore, doping concentration must be optimized — too much aluminium reduces the total heat storage capacity.

4.3 Graphite Powder — The Conductive Network Builder

Graphite has a unique layered (hexagonal) carbon structure. Heat travels very efficiently along these layers (in-plane direction), making graphite an excellent 2D heat spreader. When graphite flakes are mixed into melted wax, they disperse and orient randomly, creating an interconnected thermal network throughout the solid PCM.

Property Value Relevance
Thermal Conductivity 100–400 W/m·K 500–2000× better than paraffin; extremely effective at low concentrations
Density 2200 kg/m³ Lighter than aluminium; good for weight-sensitive EV applications
Specific Heat Capacity 0.7–0.8 kJ/kg·K Lower than paraffin — slight reduction in heat storage at high concentrations
Melting Point >3000°C Completely stable at any practical temperature
Chemical Stability Excellent Inert to paraffin; no chemical reaction during mixing

4.4 Carbon Nanotubes (CNT) — The Nano Superstar

Carbon nanotubes are cylindrical carbon structures at nanometer scale (~1–50 nm diameter, several microns long). They are essentially a graphene sheet rolled into a perfect tube. Their ballistic phonon transport (vibrations that carry heat travel through CNTs with almost zero resistance) gives them extraordinary thermal conductivity.

Property Value Relevance
Thermal Conductivity 2000–6000 W/m·K Up to 30,000× better than paraffin. Even 0.5 wt% dramatically improves the composite.
Density 1300–1400 kg/m³ Lighter than aluminium and graphite
Specific Heat Capacity ~0.7 kJ/kg·K Very low — but since CNT loading is tiny (<1%), impact on composite Cp is minimal
Tensile Strength Very High Provides structural reinforcement to PCM composite
Aspect Ratio Very High Long tubes create long conduction paths — ideal for network formation
⚠ CNT Challenge: Agglomeration

CNTs tend to clump together (agglomerate) due to van der Waals forces between tubes. Clumps are far less effective than uniformly dispersed individual tubes. This is why the project requires extended mechanical stirring (20–30 minutes) during CNT mixing.

4.5 Expected Effects on All Three Properties

Property Pure Paraffin + Aluminium + Graphite + CNT
Thermal Conductivity Lowest (0.2 W/m·K) Moderate increase Good increase Best increase
Specific Heat Capacity Highest (2–2.5 kJ/kg·K) Moderate reduction Moderate reduction Slight reduction (tiny loading)
Volumetric Expansion Highest (10–15%) Reduced most Slightly reduced Slightly reduced

Expected overall conductivity trend: Pure PCM < Aluminium < Graphite < CNT

05. Experimental Setup — Every Component Explained

The team built a custom experimental rig from scratch. Let's understand every single component and why it was chosen.

5.1 The Aluminium PCM Chamber

The main container is an aluminium box with dimensions:

Dimension Value Why This Size?
Length 23.8 cm Large enough to hold 300g of PCM with the copper coil embedded inside
Width 15 cm Provides sufficient volume for uniform heat distribution
Height 10 cm ~15% extra headspace above the PCM fill level accommodates volumetric expansion during melting
Wall Thickness 3 mm Thin enough for good heat transfer; thick enough for structural rigidity
Material Aluminium High thermal conductivity (~205 W/m·K) ensures rapid, uniform heat distribution from the coil to PCM; lightweight
🔍 The Acrylic Window

One face of the aluminium box was replaced with a transparent acrylic sheet (3–5 mm thick). This serves a critical purpose: direct visual observation of PCM melting and volume expansion. A graduated scale was fixed alongside this window to measure the rise in PCM level during melting — from which volumetric expansion is calculated.

5.2 The Copper Coil Heating Element

Instead of a direct heater touching the PCM, the design uses indirect heating via hot water circulating through a copper coil embedded inside the PCM. This is more uniform and controllable.

Parameter Value Significance
Tube Outer Diameter (OD) 4.4 mm / 6–8 mm (reported both) Small enough to coil tightly; large enough for adequate water flow
Total Tube Length 1.8 m Long coil = large surface area for heat transfer from water to PCM
Number of Turns 6 Evenly wound to distribute heat uniformly throughout PCM volume
Material Copper Very high thermal conductivity (~400 W/m·K); excellent corrosion resistance; easy to bend

5.3 Hot Water Heating & Circulation System

A 10-litre bucket of water is heated using an immersion rod heater to 70–80°C. This hot water is then pumped through the copper coil using either:

OPTION A

Active Mechanical Pump

Forced circulation with controlled flow rate (~1 L/min). More reliable and consistent.

OPTION B

Passive Thermosiphon

Buoyancy-driven natural circulation — hot water rises, cool water falls. No pump needed.

Water temperature was maintained at 75–80°C throughout experiments. A flow control valve prevented excessive turbulence.

Why water at 75–80°C? — This ensures a large enough temperature difference (ΔT ≈ 35°C) between the heat source and PCM melting point (~42°C) for adequate heat flux without overheating the system.

5.4 Temperature Sensors: DS18B20

The project uses DS18B20 waterproof digital temperature sensors — a popular, accurate, and affordable sensor.

📡 Sensor Specifications

The DS18B20 communicates via a 1-Wire digital protocol — multiple sensors can share a single data wire. Accuracy: ±0.5°C. Operating range: −55°C to +125°C. Waterproof encapsulation — safe to embed inside molten wax.

Sensor placement in the full system:

SENSOR 1

Water Inlet

Measures temperature of hot water entering the copper coil.

SENSOR 2

Water Outlet

Measures temperature of water exiting coil. ΔT across coil = heat given to PCM.

SENSOR 3

Left Wall Region

Measures temperature at the edge of the PCM — nearest to the coil.

SENSOR 4

Centre of PCM

Measures temperature at the PCM's core — farthest from heat source. Key indicator of conductivity.

A large wall-to-centre ΔT means the heat isn't penetrating well → low conductivity. A small ΔT means heat is spreading fast → high conductivity.

5.5 Insulation Layer

Three sides of the aluminium chamber are wrapped in heavy glass wool insulation (supplemented with thermocol and foam). The goal: create near-adiabatic conditions (Qloss ≈ 0) so that all the heat from the water goes into the PCM, not into the room. Since all samples are tested under identical insulation, any residual heat loss is constant and does not affect comparative results.

5.6 Arduino-Based Data Acquisition

An Arduino UNO microcontroller collects temperature data from all DS18B20 sensors in real-time. The Arduino code reads analog values, converts them to voltage, then to temperature (°C), and sends the data to a PC via serial communication every second. This creates a complete temperature log over time for each experiment.

// Conversion: Analog → Voltage → Temperature voltage = analogValue × (5.0 / 1023.0) // 10-bit ADC: 0–5V across 1024 steps temperature = voltage × 100 // LM35 principle: 10mV per °C // Data logged every 1 second, readings taken every 5 minutes for analysis

STEP 1

Melt the Wax

Heat RT42 to 70–80°C until fully liquid. Weigh to 300g accuracy on digital balance.

STEP 2

Weigh Additives

Accurately weigh required additive mass (e.g., 15g graphite for 5% sample) using digital balance.

STEP 3

Add & Stir

Slowly add additive to molten wax while stirring continuously for 10–15 minutes (20–30 min for CNT).

STEP 4

Cast

Pour liquid composite into test chamber with copper coil and T_PCM sensor already in place.

STEP 5

Record Initial Volume

Note initial height (h_i) of solid PCM via acrylic window before starting heating.

STEP 6

Cool & Solidify

Allow to cool naturally to ambient temperature before beginning each experiment.

6.3 Heating Experiment Procedure

STEP A

Activate Heater

Heat water in bucket to 75°C using immersion rod.

STEP B

Start Circulation

Activate pump; hot water flows through copper coil embedded in PCM.

STEP C

Log Temperatures

Arduino logs all sensor temperatures every 1 second; researcher records every 5 minutes for analysis tables.

STEP D

Observe Melting

Watch PCM through acrylic window — note where melting starts and how it propagates.

STEP E

Record Expansion

Measure final height (h_f) after complete melting. Note ΔT between wall and centre sensors.

STEP F

Cool & Repeat

Cool to ambient; repeat entire procedure for all 6 samples under identical conditions.

Key Physics

07. Governing Equations — Full Explanation

7.1 Total Thermal Energy Absorbed During Phase Transition

Q_total = m × C_solid × ΔT_solid [Sensible heat: solid warming to melting point] + m × L [Latent heat: solid→liquid transition] + m × C_liquid × ΔT_liquid [Sensible heat: liquid warming above melting point] Where: m = mass of PCM (kg) C_solid = specific heat of solid paraffin (~2 kJ/kg·K) C_liquid = specific heat of liquid paraffin (~2.2 kJ/kg·K) L = latent heat of fusion (~170–220 kJ/kg for RT42) ΔT = temperature rise in respective phase (°C or K)

This equation tells us the total heat the PCM can absorb during a complete heating cycle from room temperature (~25°C) to above its melting point. The middle term (m × L) is typically the dominant component — the latent heat is where PCMs really shine.

7.2 Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction (1D Steady-State)

Q = (k × A × ΔT) / x Where: Q = heat transfer rate (Watts) k = thermal conductivity of PCM (W/m·K) ← what we want to find A = cross-sectional area perpendicular to heat flow (m²) ΔT = temperature difference between two points (°C) x = distance between those two points (m)

Rearranging: k = Q×x / (A×ΔT). Since Q (heat input from water), A (chamber cross-section), and x (sensor spacing) are all constant and equal across all experiments, we know:

k ∝ 1/ΔT A smaller temperature difference (ΔT) between wall and centre = higher thermal conductivity. A larger ΔT = poorer conductor (heat piles up at wall, can't reach centre quickly).

This is how the project compares conductivity without a dedicated conductivity meter — elegant and valid.

7.3 Specific Heat Capacity — Method of Mixtures

C_PCM = [m_water × C_water × (T_in − T_out)] / [m_PCM × (T_final − T_initial)] Where: m_water = mass of hot water circulated (kg) C_water = specific heat of water = 4180 J/kg·K (known constant) T_in = water temperature entering coil (°C) T_out = water temperature exiting coil (°C) m_PCM = mass of PCM sample = 0.300 kg T_final = final PCM temperature (°C) T_initial= initial PCM temperature (°C)

This uses a simple energy balance: heat lost by water = heat gained by PCM. Since we know all water-side quantities (mass, specific heat, inlet/outlet temps), we can calculate how much heat was transferred, then back-calculate the PCM's specific heat.

7.4 Volumetric Expansion Calculation

Height Increase: Δh = h_final − h_initial % Expansion = (h_final − h_initial) / h_initial × 100 Since cross-sectional area of chamber is constant: Volume ∝ Height → % height increase = % volumetric expansion Volumetric Expansion (absolute): ΔV = A × Δh Where A = cross-sectional area of PCM chamber (length × width)

Pure paraffin wax has density ~885 kg/m³ (solid) vs ~770 kg/m³ (liquid). Density drops by ~13% on melting → volume increases by ~13–15%. Container design must always accommodate this expansion.

8.3 Melting Behaviour

Pure paraffin wax melted slowest: melting started at the walls (near the coil) and crept inward very slowly, with the centre remaining solid long after the edges were liquid. After doping:

📊 Melting Time Ranking (Fastest → Slowest)

Hybrid PCM < 1% CNT < 10% Graphite < 5% Graphite < 7% Aluminium < Pure Paraffin (slowest)

The CNT composite melted fastest because CNTs create highly efficient thermal pathways that conduct heat to the centre almost instantly. Graphite comes second — its flat flakes create broad 2D heat spreading networks. Aluminium provides moderate improvement — particles are isolated rather than forming continuous networks at 7% concentration.

8.5 Volumetric Expansion

Pure paraffin shows the highest expansion (~13–15%). Adding solid particles reduces expansion because:

REASON 1

Particle Density

Metal/carbon particles are denser than paraffin. Denser composite → less expansion per unit mass.

REASON 2

Structural Matrix

Particles form a rigid skeleton within the wax, physically constraining volumetric swelling.

REASON 3

Uniform Expansion

Better conductivity means more uniform temperature → more controlled, uniform expansion rather than localized bulging.

📊 Expansion Ranking (Most → Least)

Pure Paraffin (~13–15%) > 5% Graphite > 1% CNT > 10% Graphite > Hybrid PCM ≈ 7% Aluminium (~8–10%) — Lowest

8.6 Effect of Each Additive — In-Depth

🔷 Graphite Effect

Graphite creates 2D conductive plates throughout the PCM. At 10%, the plates are close enough to create continuous conductive networks. Higher concentration = more heat pathways = faster melting. 10% graphite shows distinctly better performance than 5%. Very cost-effective — graphite powder is cheap and readily available in India.

🟢 CNT Effect

CNTs are 1D conductors (tubes, not plates). Their extremely high intrinsic conductivity (2000–6000 W/m·K) means even at 1% by weight, they dramatically improve heat transfer. CNTs essentially "short-circuit" heat from hot regions to cool regions. The challenge: achieving uniform dispersion. The project reports careful extended stirring to minimize agglomeration.

🟡 Aluminium Effect

Aluminium particles (spherical) improve conductivity through particle-to-particle contact within the PCM. At 7%, particles are numerous enough to occasionally touch, creating metal conduction paths. Less effective than graphite or CNT for conductivity enhancement, but aluminium's key advantage is greatest reduction in volumetric expansion due to its high density and rigid structure.

8.8 Why is the Hybrid PCM Better than Pure CNT PCM?

While 1% CNT alone gives excellent conductivity, the Hybrid PCM wins overall because:

HYBRID ①

Graphite (4%)

Provides broad 2D heat spreading, uniform heat distribution, and cost reduction.

HYBRID ②

Aluminium (3%)

Reduces volumetric expansion; adds structural stability; provides additional 3D conduction paths.

HYBRID ③

CNT (0.5%)

Even 0.5% CNT contributes significantly. Less CNT = lower cost, easier dispersion, less agglomeration risk.

RESULT

Synergy

Three additives working together create a multi-dimensional conductive network: 3D (Al), 2D (Graphite), 1D (CNT).

9.2 Limitations of This Study

LIMIT 01

Lab Scale

Small 300g samples. Real EV battery pack requires kg quantities. Scaling up may change dispersion uniformity.

LIMIT 02

No Direct k Meter

Thermal conductivity was estimated comparatively via ΔT analysis, not measured with a precision instrument (Hot Disk, etc.).

LIMIT 03

Manual Stirring

Hand stirring may not achieve perfectly uniform dispersion, especially for CNTs. Ultrasonic bath or high-shear mixer would be better.

LIMIT 04

No Cyclic Testing

Long-term thermal cycling (1000s of melt-freeze cycles) was not studied. Real EV applications require cyclic stability over years.

LIMIT 05

Heat Loss

Despite insulation, heat losses could not be eliminated completely. Q_loss ≈ 0 is an assumption, not a measurement.

9.3 Specific Limitations of Each Additive

Additive Limitations
Graphite High concentrations (>10%) increase viscosity of liquid PCM; reduce latent heat storage due to volume displacement
CNT High cost; agglomeration tendency; complex mixing; nanoparticle health/safety concerns in open-air labs
Aluminium Lower enhancement per gram than CNT/graphite; long-term exposure to moisture → surface oxidation possible

9.4 Future Scope

FUTURE 01

CFD Simulation

Numerical modelling of PCM melting and heat transfer inside realistic EV battery module geometry.

FUTURE 02

Real Battery Integration

Test composite PCM wrapped around actual lithium-ion pouch/cylindrical cells during charge-discharge cycles.

FUTURE 03

Cyclic Stability

Conduct 500–1000 melt-freeze cycles and measure property degradation over time.

FUTURE 04

Expanded Graphite

Use expanded graphite (EG) instead of powder — EG forms 3D porous networks with superior conductivity at lower loading.

FUTURE 05

Encapsulation

Microencapsulate PCM in polymer shells — prevents leakage entirely, improves safety for automotive use.

FUTURE 06

Ultrasonic Dispersion

Use ultrasonic bath for CNT mixing to achieve truly uniform nanoparticle distribution without agglomeration.

9.6 Final Verdict

"The experimental study successfully demonstrated that conductive additives significantly improve the thermal behaviour of paraffin-based PCMs. The developed PCM composites show strong potential for advanced thermal management systems, particularly in Electric Vehicle battery cooling applications where efficient heat dissipation and temperature control are critically important."

This project provides a solid foundation and validated methodology for further research into advanced PCM-based thermal management systems. The identified optimal composition — RT42 + 3% Al + 4% Graphite + 0.5% CNT — balances all competing engineering requirements and is a realistic, practical candidate for commercialization in EV thermal management.
