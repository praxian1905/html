Unit 01 — Q & A

01. Thermal Conductivity W/m·K

"What does the unit of thermal conductivity signify, and how do you interpret it?"

The unit for thermal conductivity is Watts per metre-Kelvin (W/m·K). Each symbol in this compound unit carries a distinct physical meaning that, together, describe how efficiently a material moves heat.

Breaking Down Each Symbol

W
Watts

The rate of heat energy flow. 1 Watt = 1 Joule of heat transferred every second. This tells you the "power" of heat moving through the material.

m
Metres

The thickness of the material the heat must travel through. A thicker slab offers more total resistance, so k is normalised per unit thickness.

K
Kelvin

The temperature difference (gradient) between the hot face and the cold face. A larger ΔT drives more heat flow; k is normalised per 1 K of gradient.

What the Combined Unit Tells You

k = Q · x / (A · ΔT) Read as: "Watts of heat per metre of thickness per Kelvin of temperature difference" Practical meaning: A material with k = 1 W/m·K transfers exactly 1 Watt of heat through a slab 1 metre thick when the temperature difference across its two faces is 1 Kelvin (= 1 °C gap).

In plain terms: thermal conductivity tells you how quickly heat moves through a 1 m thick slice of the material for every degree of temperature difference across it. The higher the number, the faster heat travels through.

Interpreting the Value

High Value → Conductor
Copper ≈ 385 W/m·K

Heat moves very quickly

A high k means heat generated near a battery cell spreads rapidly through the surrounding material, preventing dangerous localised hotspots from forming. This is exactly what a good BTMS material must do.

Low Value → Insulator
Pure Paraffin ≈ 0.21 W/m·K

Heat stays trapped

At 0.21 W/m·K, pure paraffin wax is a poor conductor. Heat piles up near the cell surface instead of spreading to the cooler, unmelted PCM further away — a thermal bottleneck.

⚡ Why This Matters for EV Batteries

During fast charging or high-power discharge, cells can generate heat faster than the surrounding PCM can absorb it if k is too low. Adding graphite, CNT, or aluminium to paraffin raises k dramatically — the Hybrid PCM reaches ~1.05 W/m·K (+400% vs. pure paraffin), eliminating the thermal bottleneck and keeping cell surface temperatures uniform and safe.

Higher k = faster heat spread = safer cell Pure paraffin k ≈ 0.21 W/m·K — inadequate alone Hybrid PCM k ≈ 1.05 W/m·K — +400% improvement
Unit 02 — Q & A

02. Specific Heat Capacity kJ/kg·K

"What does the unit of specific heat capacity signify, and how do you interpret it?"

The unit for specific heat capacity is kilojoules per kilogram-Kelvin (kJ/kg·K). It quantifies how much thermal energy a material can store per unit of mass for each degree of temperature rise.

Breaking Down Each Symbol

kJ
Kilojoules

The amount of heat energy added to (or removed from) the material. 1 kJ = 1,000 Joules of thermal energy.

kg
Kilograms

The mass of the material being heated. Expressing it per kilogram makes Cp an intrinsic material property, independent of sample size.

K
Kelvin

The resulting change in temperature. Because this is a temperature difference, 1 K = 1 °C, so the numeric value is the same in both scales.

What the Combined Unit Tells You

C_p = Q / (m · ΔT) Read as: "kilojoules of energy needed per kilogram of material per 1 Kelvin (1 °C) of temperature rise" Practical meaning: Pure paraffin with C_p ≈ 2.3 kJ/kg·K needs 2.3 kJ to raise 1 kg by exactly 1 °C. Water (C_p ≈ 4.18 kJ/kg·K) needs 4.18 kJ for the same result — it heats up more slowly.

Think of Cp as the material's "thermal inertia" — its resistance to temperature change. A high Cp means the material soaks up a large amount of heat before its own temperature rises noticeably.

Interpreting the Value

High Cp → Thermal Sponge
Water ≈ 4.18 kJ/kg·K

Large heat storage capacity

Absorbs a large amount of heat with only a small temperature rise. Pure paraffin at ~2.3 kJ/kg·K buffers heat spikes during fast charging, preventing sudden cell temperature surges.

Low Cp → Quick Responder
Aluminium ≈ 0.90 kJ/kg·K

Small heat storage capacity

Temperature rises quickly for little heat input. Metals heat up and cool down fast — excellent for dissipating heat, but they cannot buffer large thermal pulses the way PCMs can.

🧪 The Trade-off in Composite PCMs

Adding conductive particles (graphite, CNT, aluminium) to paraffin slightly reduces the composite Cp — because metals and carbons have lower specific heats than wax. Even so, the composite Cp remains well above metals or air. The small loss in heat storage is far outweighed by the large gain in thermal conductivity, making the system both a better conductor and a capable thermal buffer.

Higher Cp = more heat absorbed per °C = gentler temperature swings Pure paraffin Cp ≈ 2.3 kJ/kg·K — strong baseline Hybrid PCM Cp ≈ 2.05 kJ/kg·K — minor reduction, fully acceptable
Unit 03 — Q & A

03. Volumetric Expansion %

"What does the unit of volumetric expansion signify, and how do you interpret it?"

The % (percentage) here is a dimensionless ratio that expresses the relative increase in volume when the PCM transitions from its solid state to its liquid state during melting. Unlike the previous two properties, there is no physical unit because it compares volume to itself.

The Calculation

% Expansion = (V_liquid − V_solid) / V_solid × 100 Example (pure paraffin wax): V_solid = 100 mL → V_liquid ≈ 113 – 115 mL % Expansion = (115 − 100) / 100 × 100 = 13 – 15 % Density relationship: ρ_solid ≈ 885 kg/m³ → ρ_liquid ≈ 770 kg/m³ Density drop ≈ 13 % on melting (same molecules, looser packing)

Three Dimensions of Interpretation

Dimension 01 — The Physical Change

Volume Grows, Mass Stays Constant

A 10% expansion means 100 mL of solid PCM becomes 110 mL of liquid. The mass is unchanged — the molecules simply rearrange into a looser packing in the liquid phase, occupying more space.

Dimension 02 — Engineering Impact

Container Design — Ullage

If a container is filled 100% with solid PCM, the pressure during melting will cause bulging, leaking, or cracking. Engineers must always leave "headspace" (ullage) — typically 15–20% free volume — to accommodate the expansion safely.

Dimension 03 — Density Relationship

Higher % = Larger Density Drop

A higher expansion percentage directly implies a significant density drop during phase change. This density change is what causes the volume increase — the same mass now occupies a bigger space.

📐 How Additives Reduce Expansion

Solid particles (graphite, CNT, aluminium) embedded in the paraffin matrix physically constrain the wax from expanding freely. Furthermore, better thermal conductivity from the additives creates a more uniform temperature distribution during melting — so the PCM melts evenly rather than in localised patches that generate pressure hotspots. Together these effects reduce the effective volumetric expansion of composite PCMs.

⚠️ Why This Matters for EV Battery Packs

In an EV battery module the PCM sits directly around the cell casing. Excessive expansion applies mechanical pressure to the cell walls, potentially deforming the casing, disrupting the electrode stack inside, or — in worst cases — contributing to a thermal runaway event. Low volumetric expansion is therefore a critical safety requirement, not a convenience.

Pure paraffin expansion ≈ 13–15% — significant design challenge Hybrid PCM expansion ≈ 8–10% — substantially reduced Lower % = safer container design = lower mechanical risk
Unit 04 — Synthesis & Comparison

04. All Three Properties — Compared & Contextualised

"What do these units of the three critical properties signify and interpret? Also compare them and their units as per the context of EV battery thermal management."

These three properties are the core design variables for any Phase Change Material used in an EV Battery Thermal Management System (BTMS). Together they determine whether a PCM will perform safely and effectively under fast charging, high-power discharge, and repeated thermal cycling over the vehicle's lifetime.

Each Property in EV Context

Property 01

Thermal Conductivity k

Unit: W/m·K

Significance: Measures the rate at which heat is conducted through the material.

In EV context: A higher k means the PCM quickly moves heat away from battery cells, preventing localised overheating. Low thermal conductivity is the primary drawback of pure PCMs, often requiring additives like graphite or CNT to raise it.

Desired direction: As high as possible. Pure paraffin (~0.21 W/m·K) is inadequate alone; the Hybrid PCM (~1.05 W/m·K) is far more effective.

Property 02

Specific Heat Capacity Cp

Unit: kJ/kg·K

Significance: Quantifies the material's "thermal inertia" — how much energy it stores per unit mass per degree.

In EV context: A high Cp allows the PCM to absorb large heat spikes during fast charging before its own temperature rises significantly — the "thermal buffer" role.

Desired direction: As high as possible, but note that additives reduce it slightly. The trade-off is always acceptable — conductivity gain far outweighs the minor storage loss.

Property 03

Volumetric Expansion

Unit: % (dimensionless)

Significance: Measures the change in physical size as the PCM melts from solid to liquid.

In EV context: Excessive expansion creates internal pressure, potentially cracking protective containers or damaging cell electrodes. Engineers must design void spaces or flexible housings to accommodate growth.

Desired direction: As low as possible. Rigid particle additives (especially aluminium) constrain the paraffin matrix and reduce expansion.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Property Unit Primary Role Desired Value Key Constraint in EV BTMS
Thermal Conductivity (k) W/m·K Heat Transport ↑ High Higher k ensures heat doesn't get "trapped" near the cell — prevents localised thermal runaway.
Specific Heat Capacity (Cp) kJ/kg·K Heat Storage ↑ High Higher Cp allows the system to buffer larger temperature spikes before phase change is complete.
Volumetric Expansion % (dimensionless) System Safety & Integrity ↓ Low Lower expansion simplifies mechanical design of the battery pack and reduces risk of container failure or cell damage.

How the Three Properties Interact

Interaction 01

k vs Cp

Adding conductive particles raises k but slightly lowers Cp — the primary trade-off. For EV BTMS, this trade-off is always worthwhile: faster heat transport prevents dangerous hotspots more effectively than a marginally higher heat storage buffer would.

Interaction 02

k vs Expansion

Higher k leads to more uniform temperature distribution during melting. Uniform melting means controlled, even expansion rather than localised bulging — so improving conductivity indirectly reduces mechanical stress from volumetric change.

Interaction 03

Cp vs Expansion

A higher Cp means the PCM absorbs more heat before completing the solid→liquid transition, delaying full expansion. Materials that melt slowly and uniformly are mechanically safer for tightly packed battery modules.

🔬

The Ideal PCM: All Three Optimised

The Hybrid PCM (RT42 + 3% Al + 4% Graphite + 0.5% CNT) achieves the best balance across all three properties: high thermal conductivity (~1.05 W/m·K) for rapid heat distribution, adequate specific heat (~2.05 kJ/kg·K) for thermal buffering, and reduced volumetric expansion (~8–10%) for mechanical safety — making it the recommended formulation for EV Battery Thermal Management Systems.
