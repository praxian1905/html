Unit 02

02. Deformation & Strengthening Mechanisms in Alloys

⚙️ Unit Overview

Explains how metals deform plastically through dislocation motion on slip systems, and then systematically covers the four principal mechanisms used to impede that motion and thereby strengthen alloys.

Dislocation Mechanics & Slip

  • Basic concepts of plastic deformation — dislocation glide as the microscopic mechanism
  • Characteristics of dislocations — stress field, strain energy, line tension
  • Slip systems — close-packed planes and directions
  • FCC: {111}⟨110⟩ — 12 slip systems
  • BCC: {110}⟨111⟩ — 12 slip systems (also {112}, {123})
  • HCP: (0001)⟨11̄20⟩ — 3 slip systems (limited ductility)
  • Slip in single crystals — Schmid's law and Critical Resolved Shear Stress (CRSS)
  • τ_CRSS = σ cos φ cos λ
  • Plastic deformation of polycrystalline materials — grain compatibility, Taylor criterion
  • Deformation by twinning — mechanism, conditions, distinction from slip

Strengthening Mechanisms

Mechanism 01 ★

Grain Size Reduction

  • Hall–Petch relationship: σ_y = σ₀ + k d^(−½)
  • Grain boundaries as dislocation barriers
  • TMCP, severe plastic deformation
Mechanism 02

Solid Solution Strengthening

  • Substitutional vs. interstitial solutes
  • Lattice strain field interactions
  • Cottrell atmosphere pinning
Mechanism 03

Strain Hardening (Work Hardening)

  • σ = K ε^n (true stress–strain)
  • Dislocation density increase
  • Recovery and recrystallisation reverse effect
Mechanism 04

Precipitation Hardening

  • Coherent precipitates — cutting
  • Incoherent precipitates — Orowan looping
  • Age hardening treatment sequence
Hall–Petch Relationship: σ_y = σ₀ + k_y × d^(−1/2) where σ₀ = friction stress (lattice resistance) k_y = Hall–Petch slope (strengthening coefficient) d = average grain diameter Schmid's Law (single crystal yield): τ_R = σ × cos φ × cos λ (resolved shear stress) Yield occurs when τ_R = τ_CRSS
Unit 03

03. Fracture in Alloys

⚠️ Unit Overview

Examines the three principal modes of failure — monotonic fracture (ductile and brittle), cyclic fatigue, and time-dependent creep — providing the fracture mechanics framework and the design parameters needed to prevent catastrophic failure.

Fundamentals of Fracture

  • Failure vs. fracture — distinction between yielding, fracture, and fatigue failure
  • Ductile fracture — necking, void nucleation, coalescence, cup-and-cone morphology
  • Brittle fracture — cleavage, transgranular/intergranular, chevron markings
  • Principles of fracture mechanics — Griffith theory, energy balance approach
  • Stress concentration — Inglis equation: σ_max = σ_nom (1 + 2√(a/ρ))
  • Fracture toughness K_Ic — plane-strain critical stress intensity factor
  • K_I = Y σ √(πa) ; fracture when K_I ≥ K_Ic
  • Units: MPa√m ; determines critical flaw size in design

Fatigue

  • Cyclic stresses — stress amplitude, mean stress, stress ratio R = σ_min / σ_max
  • The S–N curve — endurance limit (steels) vs. fatigue strength (Al alloys)
  • Crack initiation — surface stress concentrations, slip bands, inclusions
  • Crack propagation — Paris law: da/dN = C (ΔK)^m
  • Factors affecting fatigue life
  • Mean stress (Goodman, Gerber criteria)
  • Surface finish, residual stress, shot peening
  • Geometric stress concentration (notches, keyways)
  • Environmental effects — corrosion fatigue, hydrogen embrittlement

Creep

  • Generalised creep behaviour — primary, secondary (steady-state), tertiary stages
  • Stress and temperature effects — Arrhenius-type dependence
  • ε̇_ss = A σ^n exp(−Q_c / RT)
  • Larson–Miller parameter for creep-rupture life prediction
  • Creep-resistant materials — Ni superalloys, ODS alloys, ceramics
Fracture Mechanics: K_I = Y σ √(πa) Fracture when K_I ≥ K_Ic Critical crack size: a_c = (1/π)(K_Ic / Yσ)² Paris Fatigue Crack Growth Law: da/dN = C (ΔK)^m where ΔK = K_max − K_min Steady-State Creep Rate: ε̇_ss = A σ^n exp(−Q_c / RT)

04. Phase Diagrams

📊 Unit Overview

Develops the thermodynamic tools — phase rule, lever rule, tie lines — needed to read and interpret equilibrium phase diagrams, tracing microstructure evolution in isomorphous, eutectic, and iron–iron carbide systems.

Thermodynamic Foundations

  • Solubility limit, solid solutions (substitutional and interstitial), phases, microstructure
  • Phase equilibria — thermodynamic equilibrium, free energy, driving forces
  • One-component (unary) phase diagrams — water, iron polymorphism
  • Gibbs Phase Rule: F = C – P + 2   (F = degrees of freedom, C = components, P = phases)
  • Tie Line Rule — determines phase compositions at a given overall composition and temperature
  • Lever Rule — determines relative phase fractions: W_α = (C_o – C_L)/(C_α – C_L)

  • Binary isomorphous systems — Cu–Ni; liquidus and solidus curves; lever rule application
  • Development of microstructure in isomorphous alloys — equilibrium vs. non-equilibrium (coring)
  • Binary eutectic alloys — Pb–Sn, Ag–Cu; eutectic reaction: L ⇌ α + β
  • Development of microstructure: hypoeutectic, eutectic, hypereutectic compositions
  • Invariant reactions
  • Eutectic: L ⇌ α + β (const. T)
  • Eutectoid: γ ⇌ α + β
  • Peritectic: L + α ⇌ β
  • Peritectoid: α + β ⇌ γ

Iron–Iron Carbide (Fe–Fe₃C) Phase Diagram

  • Key temperatures: A₁ (727 °C eutectoid), A₃ (austenite–ferrite boundary), A_cm (austenite–cementite)
  • Phases: α-ferrite (BCC), γ-austenite (FCC), δ-ferrite (BCC), cementite Fe₃C (orthorhombic)
  • Eutectoid reaction at 0.76 wt% C: γ (austenite) → α + Fe₃C (pearlite)
  • Development of microstructure in iron–carbon alloys
  • Hypoeutectoid steels (< 0.76 wt% C) — proeutectoid ferrite + pearlite
  • Eutectoid steel — 100% pearlite (lamellar α + Fe₃C)
  • Hypereutectoid steels (> 0.76 wt% C) — proeutectoid cementite + pearlite
  • Cast irons (> 2.14 wt% C) — white, grey, malleable, ductile types
Lever Rule (example — two-phase region): Fraction of α phase: W_α = (C_o − C_β) / (C_α − C_β) Fraction of β phase: W_β = (C_α − C_o) / (C_α − C_β) Gibbs Phase Rule: F = C − P + 2 At eutectic point: C=2, P=3 → F = 0 (invariant)
Unit 05

05. Phase Transformations & Heat Treatment of Steels

🔥 Unit Overview

Covers the kinetics of solid-state transformations in iron–carbon alloys using TTT and CCT diagrams, then applies this understanding to the full suite of industrial heat treatment processes — annealing, hardening, and tempering.

Transformation Diagrams

  • Isothermal Transformation (TTT) Diagrams
  • Derived from isothermal holding experiments — start and finish curves
  • Upper nose: pearlite formation (coarse at high T, fine at low T)
  • Lower nose: bainite formation (upper bainite vs. lower bainite)
  • Martensite start (M_s) and finish (M_f) temperatures — athermal transformation
  • Continuous Cooling Transformation (CCT) Diagrams
  • Shifted to longer times and lower temperatures vs. TTT
  • Critical cooling rate to obtain fully martensitic structure
  • Mechanical behaviour of iron–carbon alloys during phase transformation
  • Hardness trends: martensite > bainite > fine pearlite > coarse pearlite > spheroidite

Heat Treatment Processes

Process 01

Annealing

Heat above A₃ / A₁, slow furnace cool. Relieves stress, refines grain, improves machinability.

Process 02

Normalising

Heat above A₃, air cool. Finer pearlite than full anneal; uniform microstructure.

Process 03

Hardening

Austenitise then quench (water/oil/air) to form martensite. High hardness and strength.

Process 04

Tempering

Reheat martensite 150–650 °C. Reduces brittleness, improves toughness; tempered martensite.

🌐 Unit Overview

Surveys the frontier of engineering materials — ceramics, composites, nanomaterials, smart alloys, biomaterials, and sustainable polymers — and contextualises materials engineering within its economic, environmental, and regulatory landscape.

Ceramics & Composites

Material 01

Ceramics

  • Structural: Al₂O₃, SiC, Si₃N₄, ZrO₂
  • Electronic: BaTiO₃, PZT, ferrites
  • Glasses and glass-ceramics
  • Applications: cutting tools, refractory, biomedical implants

Emerging and Smart Materials

  • Nanomaterials
  • Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) — sp² hybridisation, exceptional E and σ
  • Graphene — single-atom layer, highest known thermal conductivity
  • Nanocomposites, quantum dots, nano-coatings
  • Processing: sol-gel, CVD, ball milling, electrodeposition
  • Shape-Memory Alloys (SMA)
  • Thermoelastic martensitic transformation — Nitinol (Ni–Ti)
  • One-way and two-way shape-memory effect; superelasticity
  • Applications: stents, actuators, orthodontic wires

Biomaterials & Sustainable Polymers

  • Biomaterials
  • Metallic: Ti–6Al–4V, CoCrMo, 316L SS — bone implants
  • Ceramic: HA (hydroxyapatite), bioglass — bone scaffolds
  • Polymeric: UHMWPE (acetabular cups), silicone
  • Biocompatibility, osseointegration, corrosion in physiological environment
  • Recyclable polymers — PET, HDPE, PP — mechanical and chemical recycling routes
  • Biodegradable polymers — PLA, PHA, PCL — hydrolytic / enzymatic degradation

Economic, Environmental & Societal Issues

  • Life-cycle assessment (LCA) — cradle-to-grave energy and emissions
  • Material substitution strategies — lightweight alloys replacing steel
  • Critical raw materials — rare earths, cobalt, lithium supply chain risks
  • Circular economy principles in materials design
  • RoHS Directive (Restriction of Hazardous Substances)
  • EU directive 2011/65/EU — restricts Pb, Hg, Cd, Cr⁶⁺, PBB, PBDE in EEE
  • Drives solder alloy reformulation (Sn–Ag–Cu SAC alloys)
  • WEEE directive — electronic waste management and recovery
  • REACH regulation — chemical safety registration and communication
