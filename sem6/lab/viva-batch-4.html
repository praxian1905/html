Question 31 • Unit 1 (Basics of CAD)

What is the difference between geometric data and topological data in CAD?

Answer: Geometric data defines the exact spatial coordinates, dimensions, and sizes (e.g., length, radius, angle) of an object. Topological data, however, defines the invisible structural connectivity and relationships between those geometries (e.g., identifying which specific edges meet at which vertices to form a particular face). Topology ensures the solid behaves correctly when modified parametrically.

Question 32 • Unit 2 (Curves and Surfaces)

Differentiate between Analytical Curves and Synthetic Curves.

Answer: Analytical curves (like lines, perfect circles, and parabolas) are defined by simple mathematical equations and are highly rigid, making them unsuitable for free-form shapes. Synthetic curves (like Splines, Bezier curves, and NURBS) are defined by manipulating a set of control points, providing the flexibility needed to model complex, organic contours like car bodies and aircraft wings.

Question 33 • Unit 3 (Solid Modelling)

What are the essential mathematical properties required for a valid Solid Model?

Answer: A mathematically valid solid model must possess a finite, calculable volume (no infinite parts or dangling edges). It must have boundary determinism, meaning it clearly defines an unambiguous inside, outside, and boundary surface. Furthermore, it must possess structural rigidity, meaning its shape and volume remain strictly constant regardless of spatial transformations (like translation or rotation).

Question 34 • Unit 4 (CAE)

Explain the role of 'Shape Functions' in Finite Element Analysis.

Answer: In FEA, the solver only calculates exact values (like displacement or temperature) at discrete nodal points. Shape functions are mathematical interpolation polynomials used to approximate those values at any interior point within the finite element's area/volume based on the known values of the surrounding nodes.

Question 35 • Unit 5 (CAM)

What is an Automated Guided Vehicle (AGV) and what is its role in an FMS?

Answer: An AGV is a driverless, computer-controlled robotic transport vehicle that navigates the factory floor using magnetic tape, laser guidance, or floor wires. In a Flexible Manufacturing System (FMS), AGVs replace human forklifts to seamlessly transport raw materials, finished parts, and heavy tooling palettes automatically between various CNC workstations and the central storage system.

Question 36 • Unit 6 (Latest Technologies)

Define 'Cyber-Physical Systems' (CPS) in the context of Industry 4.0.

Answer: Cyber-Physical Systems represent the integration of physical mechanical hardware (like CNC machines or robot arms) with embedded digital software and internet connectivity. In Industry 4.0, CPS enables physical machines to "think" by self-monitoring, sharing data via the IoT, and making autonomous decentralized decisions directly on the factory floor.

Question 37 • Unit 2 (Curves and Surfaces)

Why is parametric representation of curves strongly preferred over non-parametric representation in CAD?

Answer: Non-parametric equations (like y = mx + c) are dependent on coordinate axes and fail when dealing with vertical slopes (which generate infinite gradients). Parametric representation solves this by using an independent parameter (usually 't', ranging from 0 to 1) to calculate x, y, and z coordinates independently. This easily allows the software to handle vertical lines, closed loops, and complex 3D spatial curves without mathematical singularities.

Question 38 • Unit 4 (CAE)

What is the fundamental difference between Static Analysis and Dynamic Analysis in CAE?

Answer: Static analysis evaluates a structure under steady, constant loads where mass inertia and damping effects are non-existent. Dynamic analysis, however, is utilized when applied loads fluctuate rapidly over time (such as high-speed vibrations, sudden impact forces, or earthquakes), making mass inertia and resonant frequencies critical factors in predicting structural failure.

Question 39 • Unit 5 (CAM)

Explain the concept of 'Group Technology' (GT) as used in CAPP.

Answer: Group Technology is a manufacturing philosophy where different parts are grouped together into a 'Part Family' because they share significant similarities in geometry or manufacturing steps. In Variant CAPP, GT allows a process planner to rapidly retrieve a standard, pre-optimized process plan for the entire family and just slightly modify it for a new part, drastically cutting down planning time.

Question 40 • Unit 6 (Latest Technologies)

How does 'Computer Aided Inspection' (CAI) typically work?

Answer: CAI utilizes precise measuring hardware, such as Coordinate Measuring Machines (CMM) or 3D optical laser scanners, to capture the exact physical dimensions of a manufactured part. This captured point-cloud data is then digitally overlaid onto the original pristine CAD solid model to automatically generate a visual deviation report highlighting any manufacturing errors or out-of-tolerance areas.
