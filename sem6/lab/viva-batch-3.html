Question 21 • Unit 1 (Basics of CAD)

Briefly explain the main application areas of CAD in modern engineering beyond simple drafting.

Answer: Beyond drafting, CAD is fundamentally used for geometric solid modelling, kinematic and dynamic simulation of assemblies, interference/collision detection, engineering analysis (serving as the direct input for FEA and CFD), and generating the foundational geometry required for CAM toolpath programming.

Question 22 • Unit 2 (Curves and Surfaces)

What does NURBS stand for, and why is it considered the industry standard for surface modelling?

Answer: NURBS stands for Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines. It is the industry standard because it provides a unified mathematical model that can accurately represent both standard analytical shapes (like perfect circles, ellipses, and cylinders) and highly complex, free-form organic surfaces (like car bodies) with exceptional precision and local control.

Question 23 • Unit 3 (Solid Modelling)

Describe the 'Sweep' representation scheme in solid modelling.

Answer: Sweep representation generates a 3D solid by taking a closed 2D planar profile and moving it along a specified trajectory in space. If the profile is moved along a straight linear path, it creates an extrusion. If it is moved around a central axis, it creates a surface of revolution (ideal for shafts and pulleys).

Question 24 • Unit 4 (CAE)

What key activities are performed during the 'Post-processing' phase of Finite Element Analysis?

Answer: Post-processing is the visualization and interpretation phase. After the computer solves the complex matrix equations, the post-processor converts raw numerical data into visual contour plots of stresses, strains, and temperature gradients. It also calculates the Factor of Safety, displays deformed shapes, and allows for animation of the structural behavior under load.

Question 25 • Unit 5 (CAM)

Define DNC and state its primary advantage over traditional isolated CNC systems.

Answer: DNC stands for Direct (or Distributed) Numerical Control. It involves networking multiple CNC machines to a central main computer/server. Its primary advantage is eliminating the need for local part-program storage on individual machines, allowing instant transfer of large toolpaths to any machine and enabling real-time shop-floor monitoring and data collection.

Question 26 • Unit 6 (Latest Technologies)

What are the basic steps involved in Reverse Engineering a physical part?

Answer: The process typically follows four steps: (1) Digitization—scanning the physical part using a CMM or 3D laser scanner to capture a massive "point cloud"; (2) Processing the point cloud to filter noise and triangulate a polygon mesh; (3) Surface fitting—draping NURBS surfaces over the mesh; and (4) Exporting the final solid parametric CAD model.

Question 27 • Unit 1 (Basics of CAD)

What is the purpose of the 'Reflection' matrix in 2D transformations?

Answer: Reflection (or mirroring) generates a mirror image of an object across a specified axis (like the X-axis, Y-axis, or an arbitrary line). It is an essential tool in CAD for rapidly designing symmetrical parts, saving time by allowing the designer to model only half (or a quarter) of the component and mirroring the rest.

Question 28 • Unit 4 (CAE)

Why is an extremely 'fine mesh' not applied uniformly across an entire CAD model during FEA?

Answer: Applying a fine mesh uniformly creates millions of microscopic elements, which exponentially increases computational solving time and memory requirements. Instead, engineers use an optimized mesh: fine only around critical areas with high stress gradients (like holes, fillets, and corners) and coarse in large, uniform geometric regions where stress is evenly distributed.

Question 29 • Unit 3 (Solid Modelling)

What is the "ambiguity problem" in wireframe modelling?

Answer: Wireframe models only store mathematical data for vertices and connecting edges, lacking any information about faces or internal solid volume. This causes the "ambiguity problem," where a 2D projection of the 3D wireframe can be visually interpreted in multiple different orientations (like an optical illusion), making it impossible to definitively know the front from the back.

Question 30 • Unit 6 (Latest Technologies)

How does Machine Learning (ML) contribute to Quality Inspection in an Industry 4.0 factory?

Answer: In smart factories, ML algorithms analyze real-time video feeds and images from high-speed factory cameras. By training on thousands of images of perfect and defective parts, the AI can automatically detect microscopic surface defects, dimensional deviations, or assembly errors much faster and more reliably than a human inspector.
