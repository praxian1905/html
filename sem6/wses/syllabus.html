Unit 01

01. Physics of Wind Power

💨 Unit Overview

Establishes the physical and statistical foundations of wind as an energy resource — from the historical development of wind technology and global capacity statistics, through aerodynamic power limits and rotor control strategies, to probabilistic wind speed modelling used in resource assessment.

History & Global Context

  • History of wind power — from windmills to modern multi-MW turbines
  • Indian wind energy statistics — installed capacity, growth trajectory, top states
  • Global statistics — leading nations, offshore vs. onshore capacity, IRENA targets

Aerodynamic Fundamentals

  • Wind physics — kinetic energy flux, Bernoulli effect, atmospheric boundary layer
  • Betz limit — theoretical maximum power extraction from wind
  • Cp_max = 16/27 ≈ 0.593 (59.3 % of available kinetic power)
  • Derived from actuator disc theory — momentum and energy balance
  • Tip speed ratio (TSR / λ) — ratio of blade tip speed to wind speed
  • λ = ω R / V_w ; optimal λ maximises Cp
  • Stall control — passive aerodynamic stall limits power at high winds; fixed pitch
  • Pitch control — active blade pitching to regulate output and protect at high winds

Power Coefficient Benchmark — Cp

Wind Speed Statistics

  • Wind speed probability distributions — Weibull distribution (k, c parameters); Rayleigh as special case
  • f(v) = (k/c)(v/c)^(k−1) exp[−(v/c)^k]
  • Cumulative distribution function (CDF) — fraction of time wind exceeds a given speed
  • Wind power density — P/A = ½ρv³ ; importance of cube law
  • Capacity factor and annual energy production (AEP) estimation from CDF
Betz Limit Derivation: P_wind = ½ ρ A v³ (available kinetic power) P_turbine = Cp × ½ ρ A v³ (extracted power) Maximum Cp = 16/27 ≈ 0.593 at axial induction factor a = 1/3 Weibull Wind Speed Distribution: f(v) = (k/c)(v/c)^(k-1) exp[-(v/c)^k] k = shape factor (≈ 2 for Rayleigh; India sites: 1.8–2.5) c = scale factor [m/s] ≈ 1.128 × V_mean

Unit 02

02. Wind Generator Topologies

⚡ Unit Overview

Reviews the electrical machine and power electronics configurations used in modern wind turbines — from fixed-speed squirrel cage induction generators to fully-rated converter systems with permanent-magnet synchronous machines — including the control strategies employed for each.

Wind Turbine Technology Overview

  • Review of modern wind turbine technologies — HAWT vs. VAWT, onshore vs. offshore
  • Fixed-speed wind turbines — SCIG direct-on-line; simple but limited energy capture
  • Variable-speed wind turbines — decoupled from grid frequency; maximum energy capture via MPPT

Generator Technologies

Generator 01 ★

DFIG — Doubly-Fed Induction Generator

  • Wound-rotor IM; stator direct to grid
  • Rotor connected via back-to-back converter (≈ 30 % rating)
  • Sub- and super-synchronous operation
  • Industry dominant — 1.5–3.5 MW class
Generator 02 ★

PMSG — Permanent-Magnet Synchronous Generator

  • High-pole-count; gearless (direct-drive)
  • Full-rated back-to-back converter
  • High efficiency; better offshore reliability
  • Preferred for offshore > 5 MW
Generator 03

SCIG — Squirrel-Cage Induction Generator

  • Fixed-speed; directly grid connected
  • Capacitor bank for reactive compensation
  • Robust, low cost, limited flexibility
Generator 04

EESG — Electrically Excited Sync. Gen.

  • Wound rotor; field current controllable
  • Full converter; medium-speed with gearbox
  • Reactive power control capability

Power Electronics & Control

  • Power electronics converters
  • Diode rectifier + DC-link + VSI (early systems)
  • Back-to-back two-level VSC (most common)
  • Multilevel converters (NPC, MMC) for large offshore turbines
  • Generator–converter configurations
  • Type 1: Fixed-speed SCIG — no converter
  • Type 2: WRIG with variable rotor resistance
  • Type 3: DFIG — partial-scale converter (rotor-side)
  • Type 4: Full-converter (PMSG or SCIG) — complete grid decoupling
  • Converter control
  • Rotor-side converter (RSC) — active power / speed MPPT control
  • Grid-side converter (GSC) — DC-link voltage, reactive power control
  • Vector control (FOC) and direct torque control (DTC)
  • Phase-locked loop (PLL) for grid synchronisation

Unit 03

03. The Solar Resource

☀️ Unit Overview

Characterises sunlight as an engineering resource — covering the electromagnetic spectrum of solar radiation, the geometric relationships between the Earth, Sun, and observer that govern irradiance on a tilted surface, and the methods used to estimate site-specific solar energy availability.

Solar Radiation

  • Introduction — solar constant G_sc = 1361 W/m² (outside atmosphere)
  • Solar radiation spectra — AM0 (extraterrestrial), AM1.5 (terrestrial standard)
  • UV (< 400 nm), visible (400–700 nm), NIR (700–2500 nm)
  • Atmospheric attenuation — Rayleigh scattering, aerosol absorption, water vapour
  • Beam (direct), diffuse, and reflected (albedo) irradiance components
  • Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) = DNI·cos(θ_z) + DHI

Solar Geometry

Geometry 01

Earth–Sun Angles

  • Declination angle δ — seasonal variation (±23.45°)
  • Equation of time — solar vs. clock time correction
  • Hour angle ω — 15°/hour from solar noon
  • Extraterrestrial irradiance vs. day of year
Geometry 02

Observer–Sun Angles

  • Solar altitude angle α and zenith angle θ_z
  • Solar azimuth angle γ_s
  • Angle of incidence on tilted surface θ
  • Optimum tilt angle ≈ latitude ± seasonal correction
  • Solar day length — sunset hour angle ω_s and day length N
  • cos(ω_s) = −tan(φ)·tan(δ) ; N = (2/15) ω_s hours
  • Estimation of solar energy availability
  • Clearness index K_T = H / H_o (measured to extraterrestrial ratio)
  • Liu–Jordan correlations for diffuse fraction
  • TMY (Typical Meteorological Year) data and satellite-derived irradiance (NASA POWER, PVGIS)
  • Perez transposition model — irradiance on tilted planes
Key Solar Geometry Relations: Declination: δ = 23.45 × sin[360(284+n)/365]° (n = day of year) Cos(θ_z) = sin(φ)sin(δ) + cos(φ)cos(δ)cos(ω) (φ = latitude, ω = hour angle) Day Length: N = (2/15) × arccos(-tan(φ)tan(δ)) hours GHI = DNI × cos(θ_z) + DHI POA = DNI × cos(θ) + DHI_sky + GHI × ρ × (1-cos β)/2

04. Solar Photovoltaic

🔆 Unit Overview

Covers the semiconductor physics and technology of photovoltaic cells — from amorphous thin films to high-efficiency mono-crystalline silicon — and extends to module/array configuration, power conditioning electronics, and MPPT algorithms that extract maximum power under varying irradiance.

Technology 01 ★

Mono-Crystalline Silicon

  • Single crystal Czochralski or FZ growth
  • Highest efficiency (20–24 %)
  • Higher cost; best space-constrained sites
  • PERC, TOPCon, HJT architectures
Technology 02

Poly-Crystalline Silicon

  • Multi-grain cast ingot; lower cost
  • Efficiency 15–20 %
  • Dominant utility-scale technology (legacy)
  • Higher temperature coefficient loss
Technology 03

Amorphous Silicon (a-Si)

  • Thin-film deposition on glass/flex substrate
  • Low efficiency (6–12 %); light-induced degradation
  • Better low-light and high-temp performance
  • BIPV, consumer electronics, low-cost applications

V–I Characteristics & PV Modelling

  • V–I characteristics of a PV cell — single-diode model
  • I = I_ph − I_o [exp(q(V+IR_s)/nkT) − 1] − (V+IR_s)/R_sh
  • Short-circuit current I_sc, open-circuit voltage V_oc, maximum power point (V_mp, I_mp)
  • Fill Factor FF = P_max / (V_oc × I_sc) ; effect of R_s and R_sh
  • Effect of irradiance (I_sc ∝ G) and temperature (V_oc decreases ≈ −2.3 mV/°C for Si)
  • PV module and array — series strings for voltage, parallel strings for current
  • Partial shading — bypass diodes, module-level power electronics (MLPE)
  • Power electronic converters for solar systems
  • String inverter, central inverter, microinverter, DC optimiser
  • DC–DC boost converter (MPPT stage) + DC–AC VSI (grid stage)

MPPT Algorithms & Converter Control

MPPT 01 ★

Perturb & Observe (P&O)

  • Perturb duty cycle; observe ΔP
  • Simple; oscillates around MPP
  • Most widely implemented
MPPT 02

Incremental Conductance (INC)

  • dP/dV = 0 at MPP → dI/dV = −I/V
  • Better tracking under rapid irradiance change
MPPT 03

Constant Voltage / Fractional V_oc

  • V_mp ≈ 0.76 × V_oc ; simple periodic sampling
  • Low complexity; some power loss
MPPT 04

AI / Model-Based MPPT

  • Fuzzy logic, ANN, sliding mode
  • Fast convergence; handles partial shading
  • Emerging in utility-scale inverters
Single-Diode PV Cell Model: I = I_ph − I_o [exp(q(V + I·R_s)/(n·k·T)) − 1] − (V + I·R_s)/R_sh where I_ph = photocurrent ∝ G (irradiance) I_o = dark saturation current ∝ T³ exp(-E_g/kT) n = ideality factor (1–2) R_s = series resistance (parasitic loss) R_sh = shunt resistance (leakage loss) Fill Factor: FF = P_mp / (V_oc × I_sc) = (V_mp × I_mp) / (V_oc × I_sc)
Unit 05

05. Network Integration Issues

🔌 Unit Overview

Addresses the technical challenges of integrating large-scale wind and solar generation into modern power systems — grid codes, fault ride-through requirements, voltage/frequency regulation, power quality, and real-world interconnection experiences including hybrid and islanded operation.

Grid Code Technical Requirements

  • Overview of grid code requirements — IEGC (India), ENTSO-E (Europe), FERC (USA)
  • Fault Ride-Through (FRT) for wind farms
  • Low Voltage Ride-Through (LVRT) — remain connected during voltage dips
  • High Voltage Ride-Through (HVRT) — tolerate voltage swells
  • Real power recovery ramp-rate requirements post-fault
  • Reactive current injection during fault (I_q ≥ 2 % rated per 1 % voltage deviation)
  • Real and reactive power regulation
  • Active power curtailment for frequency response
  • Reactive power range (power factor 0.95 lead/lag at PCC)
  • Voltage and frequency operating limits
  • Voltage range: 0.9–1.1 pu continuous ; frequency: 47.5–51.5 Hz (India)

Grid Disturbances & Power Quality

  • Solar PV and wind farm behaviour during grid disturbances — power oscillations, ramp events
  • Power quality issues
  • Voltage flicker — tower shadow, turbulence-induced power pulsation
  • Harmonics — inverter switching; IEEE 1547 THD limits
  • Voltage unbalance in large distributed PV systems
  • Inrush and energisation transients
  • Power system interconnection experiences — Denmark (offshore), Germany (Energiewende), India (ISTS)

Hybrid & Isolated Operations

Mode 01

Grid-Connected Hybrid

Wind + PV + BESS behind the meter or at utility scale. Grid provides balancing.

Mode 02

Island / Microgrid

Isolated operation with diesel backup. Frequency and voltage controlled by storage or genset.

Mode 03

Diesel Offset

Wind/PV reduces diesel consumption in remote sites; spinning reserve maintained.

Mode 04

100 % Renewable Island

Wind + PV + BESS + hydrogen storage — El Hierro model; no fossil backup.

🌐 Global Experience

Countries like Denmark routinely exceed 100 % instantaneous wind penetration, while Germany manages > 50 % renewable share. India's RE-rich states (Tamil Nadu, Rajasthan, Gujarat) face curtailment challenges — key case studies for integration strategy.

06. Solar Thermal Power Generation

🔥 Unit Overview

Surveys concentrating solar power (CSP) technologies that use optical concentration to generate high-temperature heat for thermodynamic power cycles — from parabolic troughs and central receivers to dish-Stirling systems, Fresnel reflectors, and solar ponds — with elementary thermodynamic analysis of each.

CSP Technology Overview

🔭

Parabolic Trough

Line-focus; HTF in receiver tube

🗼

Central Receiver

Point-focus; heliostat field + tower

📡

Parabolic Dish

Point-focus; Stirling engine at focus

🪞

Fresnel Reflector

Flat mirrors approximate trough

🌊

Solar Pond

Salinity gradient stores heat

CSP 01 ★ — Mature Technology

Parabolic Trough Collector (PTC)

  • Single-axis tracking parabolic mirror
  • HTF: synthetic oil (390 °C) or molten salt (550 °C)
  • Steam Rankine cycle; efficiency ≈ 14–16 %
  • Largest installed CSP base; thermal storage (TES) viable
  • Example: Rajasthan Solar Park, Ivanpah (USA)
CSP 02 ★ — High Temperature

Central Receiver (Power Tower)

  • Thousands of heliostats track sun → tower receiver
  • Concentration ratio: 300–1 000×; T > 550 °C
  • Working fluid: molten salt, steam, air (sCO₂ future)
  • Large TES enables dispatchable solar power
  • Example: Crescent Dunes, Noor III (Morocco)
CSP 03 — Modular

Parabolic Dish–Stirling

  • Two-axis tracking paraboloid; point focus
  • Stirling engine at focal point; no water needed
  • Highest solar-to-electric efficiency (≈ 30 %)
  • Modular (10–50 kW units); remote power
CSP 04 — Low Cost

Linear Fresnel Reflector (LFR)

  • Flat mirror strips approximate parabolic trough
  • Lower concentration; simpler, cheaper structure
  • Suitable for process heat and DSG (direct steam)
  • Lower land use than PTC
CSP 05 — Unique

Solar Pond

  • Salinity gradient suppresses natural convection
  • Lower zone stores heat at 70–90 °C
  • ORC (organic Rankine cycle) for power generation
  • Also useful for desalination and industrial heat

Elementary Analysis

  • Optical efficiency — intercept factor, reflectivity, transmissivity, absorptivity
  • Concentration ratio C = A_aperture / A_receiver ; maximum C_theoretical = 1/sin²(θ_sun)
  • Thermal efficiency — absorbed heat vs. optical input; radiation and convection losses from receiver
  • Overall solar-to-electric efficiency: η_sys = η_optical × η_thermal × η_cycle
  • Thermal storage — sensible heat (molten salt two-tank), latent heat (PCM), thermochemical
  • Capacity factor improvement with TES — CSP + 6–15 h storage → CF > 75 %
CSP Elementary Analysis: Optical efficiency: η_opt = ρ_mirror × τ_cover × α_absorber × γ (intercept factor) × cos θ Thermal receiver efficiency: η_th = α·G·C − U_L·(T_r − T_amb) / (G·C) Overall system efficiency: η_sys = η_opt × η_th × η_Rankine Maximum theoretical concentration ratio (2D): C_max (trough) = 1 / sin(θ_s) ≈ 215 × C_max (dish) = 1 / sin²(θ_s) ≈ 46 200 × (θ_s = 0.267° half-angle)
