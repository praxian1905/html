Safety Precautions — Milling Section

⚠ WARNING: Do NOT use any tool or machine without the supervision or guidance of a teacher.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

🥽Safety glasses must be worn at all times in work areas.
👢Sturdy footwear must be worn at all times in work areas.
👕Close-fitting / protective clothing must be worn.
💇Long and loose hair must be properly contained.
💍Rings and jewellery must NOT be worn.
🚶Do not run while working in the milling workshop.

Machine & Work Safety Rules

  • Wear appropriate safety glasses at all times.
  • Ensure the milling machine has a start/stop button within easy reach of the operator.
  • Ensure the work piece and cutter are mounted securely before taking a cut.
  • Check that work is mounted squarely.
  • Mount work in a vice that is bolted or held magnetically to the table; use proper hand tools for adjustments.
  • Hold milling cutters with a cloth to avoid being cut when handling them.
  • Move table as far as possible from cutter while setting up work — to avoid hand injury.
  • Mill the largest surface first.
  • Keep hands, brushes and rags away from the revolving milling cutter.
  • Use a vacuum, brush or rake to remove cuttings only after the cutter has stopped moving.
  • Change cutting compounds periodically.
  • Keep cutters sharpened correctly and in good condition.
  • Keep the working surface clear of scraps, tools and materials.
  • Remove cutting tools from spindle when cleaning the machine.
  • Keep the floor around the milling machine free of oil and grease.
  • Use lifting equipment when moving heavy work to or from milling machines.
  • Make sure power is off before changing cutters.
  • Always stay at the machine when it is in operation.
  • When using cutting oil, prevent splashing using appropriate splash guards. Cutting oil on floor creates a slip hazard.

Shaping Machine (Shaper)

Introduction

Shaping is a process of machining a flat surface — horizontal, vertical, inclined, concave or convex — using a reciprocating single-point tool. A shaping machine is a reciprocating-type machine tool. It was designed by James Nesmith, an Englishman, in the year 1836, specifically to produce flat surfaces.

Working Principle

The shaper machines horizontal, vertical and angular surfaces of a metal piece. A single-point cutting tool reciprocates over a stationary work piece that is rigidly gripped in a machine vice or clamped on the table. The tool is held in a tool head mounted on the ram. When the ram moves forward, material cutting begins; on the backward (return) stroke, no cutting occurs. The return stroke is faster — achieved through the quick return mechanism — reducing idle time and increasing production rate.

Quick Return Mechanism

The ram moves slower during the forward cutting stroke and faster during the return stroke, reducing idle time and increasing production. Three mechanisms achieve this:

Crank & Slotted Link Hydraulic Whitworth

(a) Crank and Slotted Link Mechanism

An electric motor drives a pinion (S) at uniform speed, which rotates a large bull gear inside the column (centre at point O). A slotted link pivoted about point K has a sliding block N inside it; P is the crank pin and OP acts as a crank. As the bull gear rotates, the sliding block circulates on the crank pin circle, providing a rocking movement to the rocker arm. The top of the slotted link is connected to the ram, so bull-gear rotation is converted into the ram's reciprocating movement.

The stroke length is the distance the ram moves forward or backward — it depends on the distance between the bull-gear centre and the sliding block centre, and is adjusted according to work length.

Ratchet & Pawl Mechanism — Automatic Table Feed

The table travels in the cross direction when the cross-feed screw rotates. The cross-feed screw is attached to a ratchet wheel. A spring-loaded pawl seats between the ratchet teeth inside a rocker arm. The bull gear drives gear B through gear A; a crank pin on a slider in gear B's slot drives the rocker arm through a connecting rod. The rocker arm rocks about the ratchet centre, and the pawl advances the ratchet in one direction only — moving the table across. To reverse the table feed direction, the pawl is turned 180°. To disengage power feed, the pawl is removed from the ratchet wheel.

Safety Precautions for Shaper

  • Work piece must be properly gripped in machine vice or clamped on the table.
  • Do not use a blunt tool.
  • Deep cuts should be avoided.
  • Stroke length must be adjusted according to the work piece length.
  • Do not touch the machining surface by hand while the ram is reciprocating.
  • A long-handle brush must be used to remove chips and scraps from the work table.
Section 3

Drilling Machine

What is Drilling?

Drilling is the process of producing a cylindrical hole in a work piece using a cutting tool called a drill.

Types of Drill

Flat Drill

Simple flat-ended drill; used for soft materials.

Straight Fluted Drill

Straight flutes; suitable for brass and bronze.

Twist Drill

Most widely used; helical flutes; two types: parallel shank & taper shank.

Oil Hole Drill

Has holes through body for coolant delivery.

Centre Drill

Produces the initial centre mark before drilling.

Step Drill

Drills holes of multiple diameters in one pass.

Core Drill

Enlarges existing holes; three or four flutes.

Twist Drill — Detailed

The twist drill consists of two main parts: shank (held in the machine) and body (the cutting unit — comprising flutes, land margin, cutting edge and drill point). By shank shape it is classified as:

  • Parallel shank twist drill — for smaller sizes; held in a chuck.
  • Taper shank twist drill — self-holding taper (Morse taper) directly into the spindle for larger drills.

The point angle for drilling mild steel, cast iron and general work is 118°. It increases for hard materials and decreases for soft materials. Twist drills are generally made from HCS (High Carbon Steel) or HSS (High Speed Steel).

Counter Sinking

Counter sinking converts a drilled hole into a cone/chamfered shape at one end using a counter-sinking bit held in the drill chuck. The tool makes a 90° angle at the hole mouth for easy tap entry and proper seating of countersunk-head screws. Counter sink tools are available in many sizes and are generally made of HSS.

Precautions While Drilling

  • Mark a dark indentation with a centre punch for proper hole location.
  • The drill bit must be properly fitted in the drill chuck or spindle.
  • Never leave the drill chuck key in the chuck.
  • Set speed and feed according to the work material.
  • Use proper coolant during drilling.
  • For large holes, first drill a small pilot hole.

Threading Tools — Taps

Taps are used to make internal threads in a drilled hole (the operation is tapping). Made of HCS or HSS. They consist of a body (with external threads for cutting) and shank (square tang for the tap wrench). Thread specifications — size, type and pitch — are marked on the shank. Precautions While Tapping

  • Drilling size must be exact as per tap size (use formula above).
  • Job must be properly clamped in the vice.
  • The drilled hole must be exactly perpendicular to the work surface.
  • Tap must be properly gripped in the tap wrench.
  • Tap must be held exactly perpendicular to the work surface.
  • Rotate one full turn clockwise, then half turn anticlockwise — this breaks and ejects chips.
  • Use proper lubricant during tapping.
  • Store taps at proper place after use.

Milling Machine

Introduction & Working Principle

The milling machine is the most versatile of all machine tools. Metal is removed from the work piece using a rotating milling cutter — a multi-point cutting tool. The work piece is fed into the rotating cutter and metal is removed in the form of chips.

Classification of Milling Machine

1. Column and Knee Type

  • Plain milling machine
  • Universal milling machine
  • Omniversal milling machine
  • Vertical milling machine

2. Other Types

  • Table type milling machine
  • Planer type milling machine
  • Special purpose milling machine

Parts of Milling Machine

Base

Heavy casting; provides stability and supports all other parts.

Elevating Screw

Raises and lowers the knee to control depth of cut.

Knee

Supports the saddle and table; moves vertically.

Saddle

Sits on the knee; carries the table; moves front-to-back (cross feed).

Table

Holds the work piece; moves left-right (longitudinal feed).

Column

Vertical body; houses spindle mechanism.

Spindle

Rotates and holds the milling cutter.

Head

Contains the spindle; may be vertical or horizontal.

Size / Specification of Milling Machine

  • Table size (length × width)
  • Maximum longitudinal, cross and vertical travel of table
  • Number of spindle speeds
  • Number of feeds
  • Spindle nose taper
  • Power required
  • Type of machine
  • Net weight and floor area

Cutter axis is vertical and generally perpendicular to the work surface. Examples: slot milling, face milling, end milling.

Indexing Head

Indexing is the method of dividing the periphery of a work piece into any number of equal parts. The indexing head (dividing head) is the attachment used. It is used for cutting gears, ratchet wheels, keyways, fluted drills, taps and reamers.

Types of Indexing Heads

  • Plain or simple dividing head
  • Universal dividing head
  • Optical dividing head

Construction of Indexing Head

  • Base: Fitted in T-slots of the milling table; supports all other parts.
  • Spindle: At the centre; has a taper hole for the live centre; supported on a swivelling block (can tilt 5° below horizontal to 10° beyond vertical); carries the worm wheel; for direct/rapid indexing, the index plate is fitted directly on the spindle nose.
  • Worm Shaft: At right angles to the main spindle; carries a single-threaded worm meshing with the worm wheel; index plate on front end; rotated by a handle.
  • Index Plate: Circular disc with concentric circles of equally-spaced holes; the crank is positioned in the required circle and rotated through a calculated amount; the sector arm eliminates the need to count holes each time.
  • Driven Shaft: Parallel to spindle; perpendicular to worm shaft.
  • Dead Centre: Work is held between the spindle centre and this dead centre; can slide to required location.

Working Principle of Dividing Head

When the crank is rotated through a required number of holes in the index plate, the work rotates a proportional amount via the worm and worm wheel. For differential indexing, a gear train between the main spindle and driven shaft simultaneously rotates the index plate slightly — allowing divisions that are not directly achievable with the index plate holes. In differential indexing, the index plate lock pin is released.

Indexing Methods

  • Direct or rapid indexing
  • Plain or simple indexing
  • Compound indexing
  • Differential indexing
  • Angular indexing

Safety Precautions for Milling Machine

  • Ensure cutter and arbor are securely fixed.
  • Use cutters that are correctly ground and in good condition.
  • Move table with work as far from cutter as possible while setting up.
  • Check speed and feed — must be appropriate.
  • Keep hands away from the work during machining.
  • Do not strike milling cutters.
  • Clean the machine after work; remove chips with a brush.

Gear Manufacturing

Basic Purpose of Gears

Gears transmit power and motion positively (without slip) between parallel, intersecting or non-intersecting non-parallel shafts — with or without change in direction or speed of rotation.

General Applications

  • Speed gearbox, feed gearbox and kinematic units of machine tools
  • Speed drives in textile, jute and similar machinery
  • Automobile gear boxes
  • Metal forming machine speed and feed drives
  • Mining and tea-processing machinery
  • Heavy-duty gear boxes in cement, sugar, crane and conveyor industries
  • Precision instruments, clocks and watches
  • Industrial robots and toys

Types of Gears

By Configuration

  • External gear — teeth on outer surface; meshes externally
  • Internal gear — teeth on inner surface; meshes internally with a pinion

By Axes of Transmission

  • Spur gears (parallel shafts) — straight-toothed or helical (single or double helical/herringbone)
  • Bevel gears (intersecting shafts) — straight-toothed, spiral or hypoid
  • Worm and worm wheel (non-parallel, non-intersecting shafts)
  • Hypoid and spiral gears

By Pattern of Motion

  • Rotation to rotation — wheel type gears (straight or helical)
  • Rotation to translation or vice versa — rack and pinion

Gear Manufacturing Processes

CategoryProcesses
FormingCasting, Sintering, Injection moulding, Extruding, Cold drawing, Stamping, Preforming, Forging
Machining — RoughingForm milling, Rack generation, Gear shaping, Hobbing
Machining — FinishingShaving, Grinding, Burnishing, Lapping, Honing

Gear Milling (Forming)

Gear milling can produce all types of gears. A form-milling cutter selected for a specific gear type and module is used. The cutter's cutting edge has a shape identical to the space between the gear teeth. The gear blank periphery is divided into equal parts (equal to the number of teeth) using indexing. After each tooth space is cut, the indexing head advances the blank by one tooth pitch.

Disadvantages of Gear Milling
  • Slower than other gear generation methods (teeth cut one by one).
  • Not suitable for large batch sizes.
  • Economical only for small batch sizes; other methods have high capital cost.

Gear Shaping

Uses a gear-shaped cutting tool (pinion-shaped or rack-shaped cutter) that reciprocates axially across the gear blank while the blank rotates around the cutter — a true shape-generation process. The gear-shaped tool cuts itself into mesh with the gear blank. Accuracy is good, but errors in one tooth of the cutter are directly transferred to the gear. Can also cut internal gears.

Gear Hobbing

A machining process in which gear teeth are progressively generated by a series of cuts with a helical cutting tool (hob). All motions are rotary — the hob and gear blank rotate continuously as if meshing together, until all teeth are cut. This is a highly productive continuous generating process.

Gear Finishing

Surface finish and dimensional accuracy from roughing processes may not be sufficient for precision applications. Finishing operations include:

Shaving Grinding Honing Lapping Burnishing

During shaving, the cutting teeth of the shaving cutter interact with the mating gear to provide high accuracy and surface finish.

Drilling, Tapping, Recessing & Chamfering on a Block

Aim: To prepare a job consisting of drilling, tapping, recessing and chamfering operations on milling/shaper and drilling machine.

Material: MS Flat — 72 × 26 × 15 mm

Machines Used: Milling machine, Shaper, Drilling machine

Tools & Equipment Required

  • Vernier caliper (200 mm)
  • Vernier height gauge (300 mm)
  • Flat file (150 mm)
  • Shell end mill cutter
  • Single-point cutting tool
  • Double-end spanner
  • Surface plate, Angle plate
  • Centre punch, Centre drill
  • Twist drill (∅6.5 mm)
  • Drill chuck key
  • Counter sinking tool
  • Drill chuck and sleeve MT4, Drift
  • Tap set M8 × 1.25 mm

Drawing Dimensions

  • Overall dimensions: 72 mm × 26 mm × 15 mm
  • Recess to maintain thickness: 13 mm
  • Hole: ∅6.5 mm pilot, tapped to M8
  • Chamfer: 1 × 45° on all sides except bottom

Step-by-Step Procedure

1
Mark the work piece for drilling ∅6.5 mm as per drawing.
2
Hold the work piece on punching plate and mark a centre punch at the intersection point for the drilling operation.
3
Hold the work piece in the drilling vice and clamp the vice on the drill machine table.
4
Fit the drill chuck in the spindle of the drill machine and hold the centre drill in the drill chuck.
5
Set the machine RPM appropriate for the centre drill.
6
Make a centre drill hole at the punched mark.
7
Remove centre drill; fit the ∅6.5 mm twist drill in the chuck.
8
Drill the ∅6.5 mm hole for the subsequent tapping operation.
9
Remove drill; fit the counter-sinking tool in the drill chuck.
10
Remove burr and sharp edges from the drilled hole using the counter-sinking tool.
11
Remove counter-sinking tool and work piece from the drilling vice.
12
Mark the work piece for the recessing operation as per drawing.
13
Hold work piece in the machine vice of milling machine/shaper; mount the shell end mill cutter / single-point cutting tool on the tool holder.
14
Set the RPM and feed of the machine for the recessing operation.
15
Remove extra material from the work piece; maintain thickness 13 mm as per drawing.
16
Remove work piece from shaper/milling; hold in bench vice for tapping M8.
17
Use M8 tap set (taper → intermediate → bottoming) for tapping the M8 hole.
18
Chamfer all edges (1 × 45°) using a flat file (150 mm).
19
Punch the roll number on the side; remove all burrs.

Precautions

  • Hold the work piece properly in the machine vice.
  • Use cutters and single-point cutting tools that are correctly ground and in good condition.
  • Move the table as far as possible from the cutter while setting up.
  • Check speed, feed and depth of cut — must be correct.
  • Keep hands away from the work during machining.
  • Do not strike over milling cutters, single-point tools, drills or tap sets.
  • Use proper coolant during drilling and tapping.
  • Clean the machine after completion and remove chips with a brush.

Single Point Brazed Tool with Carbide Tip on MS Shank

Aim: To prepare a single-point brazed tool with carbide tip on a mild steel shank involving milling operation.

Material: MS flat SQ. 25 × 160 mm and a carbide bit (insert).

Machine Used: Vertical milling machine

Tools & Equipment Required

  • Vernier caliper (200 mm)
  • Flat file (150 mm)
  • End mill cutter
  • Double-end spanner

About the Tool (37 Type / 38 Type)

The single-point brazed tool consists of a mild steel shank with a carbide tip (insert) brazed onto it. The 37 Type is a right-hand tool; the 38 Type is left-hand. Key dimensions for the 25 × 160 mm shank: shank width W = 25 mm, height H = 25 mm, length L = 160 mm, overhang E = 10 mm. The carbide insert dimensions: A = 24 mm, C = 6 mm, R = 1 mm.

Step-by-Step Procedure

1
Cut the MS flat to SQ. 25 mm in 160 mm length on the power hacksaw.
2
Remove burr and sharp edges from all surfaces using the flat file.
3
Hold the work piece in the machine vice of the milling machine.
4
Set the end mill cutter on the tool holder of the milling machine.
5
Remove extra material from the shank as per the carbide insert dimensions (to create the seat/pocket for brazing).
6
Remove the end mill cutter from the tool holder and the work piece from the machine vice.
7
Send the machined shank to the welding section for tip (insert) brazing.

Precautions

  • Hold the work piece and cutting tool properly on the machine.
  • Use milling cutters that are correctly ground and in good condition.
  • Move table as far from cutter as possible while setting up.
  • Check speed, feed and depth of cut — must be correct.
  • Keep hands away from the work during machining.
  • Clean the machine after completion and remove chips with a brush.
Result
The machined shank for the single-point brazed tool with carbide tip has been completed as per drawing. The shank is then forwarded to the welding shop for brazing of the carbide insert.
