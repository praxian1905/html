01. Fundamentals of Milling

Objectives of the Chapter

After studying this chapter, the reader should be able to:

  • Understand the characteristic features of the milling process.
  • Know the various types of milling machines and their applications.
  • Select appropriate milling cutters based on the job.
  • Understand different types of milling operations.
  • Select suitable work-holding devices for a given part.
  • Understand indexing methods for parts.
  • Grasp the mechanics of milling and how process variables affect performance.
  • Calculate machining time and power requirements.
  • Set up special operations on a milling machine for specific surfaces.

Introduction to Milling

Milling is one of the most widely used manufacturing processes after turning (lathe operations).

Key difference from turning:

  • In turning (lathe): Single-point cutting tool + rotating workpiece.
  • In milling: Multipoint rotating milling cutter + reciprocating or linear feed of the workpiece.

The tool is called a milling cutter, and the axis of rotation is usually perpendicular or parallel to the feed direction.

Fig. 7.1 typically shows a schematic of peripheral milling with the cutter removing material (chip) from the workpiece as it feeds.

Characteristics of the Milling Process

Milling has several distinctive features that make it highly productive:

Feature Description Advantage / Implication
Interrupted Cutting Each cutting edge removes material only during part of its rotation. Cutting edge cools between cuts -> Cooler operation than continuous turning -> Allows higher material removal rates.
Small Size of Chips Chips are small, but multiple teeth cut simultaneously. Large volume of material removed quickly -> Often finished in single pass.
Variation in Chip Thickness Chip thickness varies from zero to maximum (or vice versa) during each tooth engagement. Causes cyclic variation in cutting forces -> Can excite machine vibrations -> Affects tool life and surface finish.
⚡ Key Insight

Milling is an interrupted cutting process, which allows higher material removal rates while demanding attention to force variation and chatter.

Other highlights:

  • Extremely versatile machine tool.
  • Suitable for both job shops/toolrooms and quantity production.
  • Wide variety of accessories and tools available.
  • Typical tolerance: ± 0.050 mm.

Types of Milling Machines

Milling machines are classified based on size, application, and degree of automation or flexibility:

4.1 Knee and Column Type (General Purpose)

Most common and flexible type.

  • Horizontal - Spindle is horizontal.
  • Vertical - Spindle is vertical.
  • Universal - Can be tilted or swiveled for complex angles.
  • Turret type - Highly flexible head movement.

Applications: All types of work, including batch manufacturing. Many attachments available.

4.2 Production (Bed) Type

  • Simplex (one spindle)
  • Duplex (two spindles)
  • Triplex (three spindles)

Designed for high-volume production with large batch sizes. Less flexible but higher productivity.

4.3 Plano Millers

  • Used for very large workpieces (table travel in meters).
  • Heavy-duty machines.

4.4 Special Type Machines

  • Rotary table
  • Drum type
  • Copy milling (die-sinking)
  • Keyway milling
  • Spline-shaft milling

These are dedicated machines for specific, repetitive, or complex operations not easily done on general-purpose machines.

Part 2

02. Machine Types and Construction

Knee and Column Milling Machine

This is the most commonly used milling machine due to its high flexibility and easy setup.

Key Construction Features (Horizontal Knee and Column Type)

  • Knee: Houses the feed mechanism. Supports the saddle and table. Can move up and down (Z-axis) on dovetail guides on the column.
  • Table: Has T-slots for work holding. Moves longitudinally (X-axis) on the saddle.
  • Saddle: Moves transversely (Y-axis) on the knee.
  • Column: Massive structure at the back. Houses the motor, spindle gearbox, and power train.
  • Spindle and Arbor:
    • Spindle located at the top of the column.
    • Arbor (shaft) holds the milling cutters.
    • Supported by an over-arm with bearing to handle heavy cutting forces.
    • Cutters are positioned using hardened collars and clamped with a draw bar and nuts.
  • Feeds:
    • Longitudinal and cross feeds can be automatic (via lead screw).
    • Vertical (knee) movement is usually manual.

Machine specification parameters:

  • Size of the table (working area).
  • Amount of table travel (X, Y, Z movements).
  • Horsepower of the spindle (1-3 hp for small machines, up to 10-50 hp for production machines).

Vertical Knee and Column Type

  • Spindle is mounted vertically.
  • Ideal for shank-mounted cutters such as end mills.
  • Easier workpiece setup and better visibility of the cutting operation.
  • More flexible than horizontal type.
  • Vertical head can often swivel, allowing angled cutting.
  • Excellent for toolroom work like machining complex die cavities.

Universal Knee and Column Machine

  • Table can be swiveled plus or minus 45 degrees in the horizontal plane.
  • Suitable for milling spur gears, helical gears, worm gears, and cams.

Bed-Type (Production) Milling Machines

These machines are designed for high metal removal rates and large-batch production.

Why bed-type?

  • Conventional knee-type machines may develop chatter if depth of cut is increased too much.
  • Bed-type machines are more rigid (less versatile) to allow heavier cuts.

Key features:

  • Table is fixed directly on the bed and has only longitudinal movement.
  • Spindle head moves along the column to provide cutting action.
  • Simplex: One spindle head.
  • Duplex: Two spindle heads (can machine both sides of a workpiece simultaneously).
Machine Type Main Use Flexibility Rigidity / Metal Removal Key Features
Knee and Column (Horizontal) General purpose High Medium Arbor + over-arm support
Knee and Column (Vertical) Toolroom, dies, complex parts Very High Medium Swivel head, end mills
Universal Knee and Column Gears, cams, angular work High Medium Swivel table
Bed-Type (Simplex/Duplex) Production, high volume Low Very High Fixed table, heavy cuts
Part 3

03. Cutters and Milling Methods

Milling Cutters

Milling cutters are multi-point rotary tools. Their wide variety gives milling machines great versatility.

Classification of Milling Cutters

  1. Based on Construction
    • Solid cutters
    • Inserted tooth type (replaceable teeth)
  2. Based on Mounting
    • Arbor mounted (for horizontal machines)
    • Shank mounted (mainly for vertical machines)
    • Nose mounted
  3. Based on Rotation
    • Right-hand (counter-clockwise)
    • Left-hand (clockwise)
  4. Based on Helix
    • Right-hand helix
    • Left-hand helix

Materials: High-Speed Steel (HSS) or Cemented Carbides (brazed tip or indexable inserts).

Type Mounting Main Use Key Features
Plain / Slab Milling Arbor Flat surfaces Cylindrical, teeth on periphery. Light-duty (many teeth), Heavy-duty (fewer teeth, more chip space). Helical versions for better finish.
Side and Face Arbor Side milling + slotting Teeth on sides + periphery. Versatile.
Staggered Tooth Arbor Deep slots Alternate helix teeth -> more chip space.
Half-Side Arbor One-sided machining Positive rake, smoother cut, less power.
Slitting Saw Arbor Cutting off, thin/deep slots Very thin, saw-like.
Special Form Cutters Arbor Specific profiles Angular, T-slot, Woodruff keyseat, Dovetail, Gear, etc.
End Mills Shank Slots, keyways, pockets, contours Most versatile. Can plunge (slot drills). Various designs: 2/4 flute, ball nose, corner radius, shell end mills.
Face Milling Cutters Nose Large flat surfaces Large diameter, carbide inserts preferred. Teeth on face + periphery.

Important design variations in end mills:

  • Straight or tapered shank.
  • Center-cutting (slot drills) vs non-center-cutting.
  • Ball nose (for 3D contours), corner radius.
  • Shell end mills (large size, no shank).

Selection guidelines:

  • Prefer standard tools.
  • Use shortest overhang.
  • Cutter diameter is about 30% larger than width of cut (face milling).
  • Close pitch (many teeth) as first choice.
  • Coarse pitch for long overhangs or unstable conditions.
  • Extra close pitch for short-chipping materials.

Straight vs Helical Teeth:

  • Straight teeth: One tooth at a time, high force variation -> more vibration.
  • Helical teeth: Multiple teeth in cut, smoother force variation -> better finish and stability.
🔬 Process Choice

Helical cutters and climb milling generally deliver smoother operation and better surface finish, provided machine rigidity is adequate.

Up Milling vs Down Milling

Feature Up Milling (Conventional) Down Milling (Climb)
Cutter vs Feed Direction Opposite directions Same direction
Chip Thickness Starts at zero -> increases to maximum Starts at maximum -> decreases to zero
Force on Workpiece Tends to lift workpiece Pushes workpiece down (better holding)
Surface Finish Poorer (feed marks, rubbing at start) Better finish
Power Requirement Higher Up to 20% lower
Safety Safer, inherently stable Risk of workpiece being pulled in (needs backlash eliminator)
Best For Roughing, castings, hard scale materials Finishing, thin or frail parts, deep slots

Advantages of down (climb) milling:

  • Better surface finish
  • Longer tool life
  • Higher feeds possible
  • Good for thin parts

Disadvantages of down milling:

  • Requires rigid machine with backlash eliminator
  • Not suitable for materials with hard outer scale

Work Holding

Milling machine tables have precision T-slots for clamping.

Common work-holding devices:

  • Vice - Most common for small or regular workpieces (many jaw types available).
  • Universal Chuck - For round workpieces (end slots, splines).
  • Direct clamping on table using T-slots, straps, clamps, and bolts.
  • Angle plates, V-blocks, sine bars, fixtures (for complex or production work).
Part 4

04. Set-ups, Indexing, and Mechanics

Work Holding (Continued)

  • Fixtures: Most common in production milling. They reduce setup time and provide high locational accuracy and repeatability.
  • Clamps: Used for large or irregular workpieces. Types include plain slotted clamps, U-clamps, gooseneck clamps, finger clamps, and step blocks with T-bolts.

Important rules for clamping:

  • Workpiece must not shift under cutting forces.
  • Clamping force should not distort the workpiece.
  • Proper support and correct placement are essential.

Milling Set-ups / Methods

Method Description Main Advantage Typical Application
String / Line Milling Multiple small workpieces placed in a line on the table. Reduces approach distance and idle time Small identical parts
Abreast / Reciprocal Milling Special fixture indexes 180 degrees. One part machined while other is loaded. Machine keeps cutting (no idle time) Batch production
Rotary / Circular Milling Multiple fixtures on a rotary table. Continuous cutting, high productivity High volume
Gang Milling Multiple cutters mounted together on one arbor (e.g., slab + two side cutters). Several surfaces machined simultaneously Production (inverted U, complex profiles)
Straddle Milling Special case of gang milling using only side and face cutters. Machining two parallel surfaces at once Slots, steps
⚠ Note

In gang milling, cutting speed is limited by the largest diameter cutter. Prefer cutters of similar size.

Dividing Head (Indexing Head)

One of the most important attachments for milling. Used for accurate angular positioning and dividing the workpiece into equal or unequal parts (e.g., gears, splines, keyways).

Basic principle:

  • Worm gear ratio is usually 40:1 -> 40 turns of index crank = 1 full revolution of workpiece.
  • Index plates with circles of holes allow fractional rotations.

Standard index plates:

  • Brown and Sharpe: Plate 1 (15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20), Plate 2 (21, 23, 27, 29, 31, 33), Plate 3 (37, 39, 41, 43, 47, 49)
  • Cincinnati / Parkinson: More hole circles available.

Simple / Plain Indexing

Direct method using index crank + plate.

Formula:

Index crank turns = 40 / (Number of divisions)

Examples:

  • 20 divisions (gear): 40/20 = 2 full turns
  • 6 divisions: 40/6 = 6 + 2/3 turns -> Use appropriate hole circle (e.g., 10 holes in 15-hole circle)
  • 28 divisions: 1 full + 9 holes in 21-hole circle

Compound Indexing

Used when simple indexing cannot achieve the required divisions.

  • Combine two movements: one with crank, second by rotating the index plate itself.
  • Involves addition or subtraction of fractions.

Example 7.3 (77 divisions): 9 holes in 21-circle + 3 holes in 33-circle.

Example 7.4 (141 divisions): 26 holes in 39-circle - 18 holes in 47-circle.

Angular Indexing

Indexing based on angle instead of number of divisions.

Since 1 crank turn = 9 degrees (360 degrees / 40):

Formula:

Crank turns = Required angle / 9 deg

Examples:

  • 41 degrees -> 4 full turns + 10 holes in 18-hole circle
  • 15 degrees 30 minutes -> 1 full + 13 holes in 18-hole circle

Differential Indexing

Advanced, semi-automatic method for numbers not possible with simple or compound indexing.

  • Index plate is unlocked and connected via change gears to the spindle.
  • Plate rotates automatically to compensate for the approximate indexing used.
  • Requires calculation of gear ratio and idler gear selection.

Useful when required divisions do not factor nicely with available hole circles.

Milling Mechanics (Slab Milling)

  • The path of the cutting tooth is a trochoid.
  • Chip thickness varies continuously.
  • Up milling: Chip length slightly longer due to sliding at entry.
  • Down milling: Slightly shorter chip length.

Key insight: Helical cutters and down milling produce smoother operation and better surface finish due to more continuous cutting action.

Part 5

05. Mechanics, Time, and Power

Slab Milling (Peripheral Milling)

  • The path traced by a cutting tooth is a trochoid.
  • Chip thickness varies from zero to a maximum value.

Key formulas:

Maximum chip thickness:

t_max = (2 f sqrt(d (D - d))) / (N Z D)

Mean chip cross-sectional area (straight teeth):

A_mean = (W f sqrt(d (D - d))) / (N Z D)

Example 7.11: A 100 mm dia., 8-tooth cutter at 30 m/min, 4 mm depth, 150 mm/min feed, 120 mm width -> Mean chip area = 4.617 mm^2.

Face Milling

Chip thickness analysis depends on symmetrical or unsymmetrical cutting.

Symmetrical cut:

Mean chip thickness:

t_mean = (2 f_t sin(phi_c / 2)) / phi_c

Mean area:

A_mean = (2 W f_t sin(phi_c / 2)) / phi_c

Unsymmetrical cut:

Mean area:

A_mean = (2 W f_t (cos phi_2 - cos phi_1)) / phi_c

Milling Time Estimation

Cutting speed:

V = (pi D N) / 1000 (m/min)

Approach allowance (A):

  • Slab milling: A = sqrt(d (D - d))
  • Face milling:
    • If W >= D/2: A = D/2
    • If W < D/2: A = sqrt(W (D - W))

Time for one pass:

Time = (L + 2A) / (f Z N) (minutes)

where L = length of workpiece, f = feed per tooth (mm/tooth).

Example 7.12 (Slab Milling): C50 steel, 100x250 mm, 2 mm depth, 100 mm HSS slab cutter -> Time about 2.633 minutes.

Example 7.13 (Face Milling): Alloy steel, 115x250 mm, 6 mm depth, 150 mm carbide face mill -> Time about 1.06 minutes.

Reference data: Table 7.1 provides recommended speeds and feeds for HSS and Carbide cutters for various materials.

Milling Power Estimation

Material removal rate (MRR):

Q = (f_m w d) / 60000 (cm^3/s)

where f_m = f x Z x N (mm/min).

Power:

P_m = K_p Q C W (hp)
  • K_p = Power constant (Table 7.2) - depends on material and hardness.
  • C = Feed factor (Table 7.3) - depends on feed per tooth.
  • W = Tool wear factor (Table 7.4) - 1.10 for slab/end milling, higher for heavy face milling.

Example 7.14: Same face milling job as above -> Power required about 5.84 hp.

Milling Troubleshooting

Problem Possible Causes and Remedies
Chatter (vibration) Low rigidity, high depth, dull cutter, no coolant, straight teeth -> Reduce depth, use helical cutter, sharpen tool.
Poor Dimensional Accuracy High forces causing deflection, chips in locating surfaces -> Reduce depth, improve chip flushing.
Low Tool Life High forces, insufficient coolant -> Reduce depth, apply cutting fluid.
Poor Surface Finish High feed, dull cutter, low speed, few teeth -> Lower feed, sharpen cutter, increase teeth/speed.
Part 6

06. Special Operations and Consolidated Summary

Spiral (Helical) Milling

Used to cut helical grooves (e.g., helical gears, flutes, worms) using the dividing head.

Principle:

  • Link dividing head worm to table lead screw via change gears.
  • Lead of machine = 40 x Lead of table feed screw.

Gear ratio:

Gear on index shaft / Gear on table lead screw = Lead of machine / Lead of helix
  • Idler gear controls hand of helix (right or left).

Example 7.15: Table lead screw = 5 mm, helix lead = 480 mm -> Calculate suitable gear train.

Cam Milling

Used for machining plate cams with uniform rise or fall.

Set-up:

  • Performed on a universal milling machine with a vertical milling attachment.
  • Uses a long end mill whose diameter equals the follower diameter of the cam.
  • Dividing head holds the cam blank and is tilted at an angle alpha.
  • The end mill is also inclined at the same angle alpha so that cutter axis remains parallel to cam axis.
  • Table movement + dividing head rotation generates the cam profile.

Formula for setting angle:

sin(alpha) = (Lead of the cam x Gear ratio) / (Machine lead)
  • When alpha = 0 degrees, the cam has zero lead (circular profile).
  • Gear ratio between dividing head and table lead screw controls the rise/fall rate.

Example 7.17: 10 mm rise in 60 degrees rotation, machine lead screw = 5 mm -> Machine lead = 200 mm, Cam lead = 60 mm, Gear ratio = 2 -> alpha = 36 degrees 52 minutes

✅ Summary

Milling is a versatile, high-productivity multipoint machining process characterized by interrupted cutting. Its strength lies in the variety of machines, cutters, and indexing methods available for precision work.

Consolidated Chapter Highlights

  • Advantages: High productivity, versatility, good accuracy (about ±0.05 mm), cooler operation than turning.
  • Challenges: Variable chip thickness, cyclic forces, possibility of chatter.
  • Machines: Knee and Column, Bed Type, Plano Millers, and Special Machines.
  • Cutters: Slab, Side and Face, End Mills, Face Mills, Form Cutters.
  • Methods: Up vs Down milling, indexing (simple, compound, angular, differential).
  • Calculations: Chip thickness, machining time, and power estimation.
