Question A

(a) What is meant by mean piston speed? Explain its importance.

Mean piston speed is the average velocity of the piston as it moves back and forth in the cylinder. Since piston speed varies from zero at Top/Bottom Dead Centers to a maximum near mid-stroke, the average is calculated using the stroke length (L) and engine speed (N).

Sp = 2LN Where: Sp = Mean piston speed (m/s) L = Stroke length (m) N = Engine speed (rev/s)
⚡ Importance

Mean piston speed is a critical limiting parameter for engine design. High mean piston speeds lead to excessive inertia forces (causing mechanical stress on the connecting rod and bearings) and increase the friction between the piston rings and cylinder wall. It also heavily dictates the gas flow velocities through the valves, which limits volumetric efficiency.

Question B

(b) What is the use of air-standard cycle analysis?

Air-standard cycle analysis provides a highly idealized theoretical model of an engine cycle by assuming air as a perfect working fluid. Its primary uses are:

USE 01 ★

Upper Limit Baseline

It establishes the absolute maximum theoretical thermal efficiency an engine design could achieve, serving as a baseline for comparing actual engine performance.

USE 02

Isolating Variables

It simplifies complex thermodynamic calculations, allowing engineers to isolate and understand how changes in basic parameters (like compression ratio) affect efficiency, without the complications of actual combustion chemistry.

Question C

(c) How does exhaust temperature and mean effective pressure affect engine performance?

PARAMETER 1

Mean Effective Pressure (MEP)

MEP is a theoretical constant pressure that would produce the same work per cycle as the actual varying pressure. A higher MEP directly correlates to a higher power output for a given engine displacement, indicating excellent cylinder packing and combustion efficiency.

PARAMETER 2

Exhaust Temperature

A high exhaust temperature indicates that a significant portion of combustion heat was rejected rather than converted into work, signalling lower thermal efficiency, delayed burning, or an excessively rich mixture. It acts as an indicator of waste energy.

Question D

(d) Define volumetric efficiency and discuss affecting factors.

✓ Definition

Volumetric efficiency (ηv) is the ratio of the actual mass of air inducted into the cylinder during the intake stroke to the theoretical mass of air that would fill the swept volume at ambient temperature and pressure.

Factors affecting volumetric efficiency include:

Engine Speed Valve Timing & Size Intake Manifold Temp Exhaust Back-pressure
  • Engine Speed: At very high speeds, friction and flow resistance increase, dropping ηv.
  • Intake Temperature: Hotter intake tracts reduce air density, directly lowering the mass of air inducted.
  • Valve Geometry: Smaller valves or poor timing restrict airflow, throttling the incoming charge.
Question E

(e) Explain why a rich mixture is required for idling.

During idling, the engine operates at low speeds with the throttle valve nearly closed. This creates two distinct problems that require a rich mixture (high fuel-to-air ratio) to solve:

⚡ Exhaust Gas Dilution

Because of the closed throttle, a high vacuum is formed in the intake manifold. During valve overlap, a significant amount of burned residual exhaust gas is sucked back into the cylinder. A richer mixture ensures enough fuel molecules are present among this inert gas to sustain combustion.

Additionally, low air velocities at idle lead to poor fuel atomization and vaporization, meaning extra liquid fuel must be supplied to yield a combustible vapor near the spark plug.

Question F

(f) Why high fuel pressure is required in modern injection systems?

Modern Direct Injection (DI) systems in both SI and CI engines require incredibly high fuel pressures to optimize combustion. The sequence of benefits is as follows:

PHASE 01

Superior Atomization

High pressure breaks the injected liquid fuel into much smaller micro-droplets, drastically increasing the surface area.

PHASE 02

Better Penetration

The high momentum allows the fuel jet to penetrate deeply into the highly compressed, dense air inside the cylinder.

PHASE 03

Rapid Vaporization

The micro-droplets absorb heat rapidly, vaporize, and mix with oxygen instantly, reducing emissions and increasing power.

Question G

(g) What is delay period and what are the factors that affect it?

In a CI engine, the delay period (ignition delay) is the time interval between the start of fuel injection into the combustion chamber and the actual onset of auto-ignition.

Factor Effect on Delay Period
Compression Ratio Higher compression ratio increases air temperature, decreasing the delay period.
Cetane Number A higher cetane number indicates better ignition quality, decreasing the delay period.
Intake Air Temp/Pressure Higher initial temperature and pressure decrease the delay period.
Engine Speed Increasing speed reduces available physical time, effectively increasing the delay period in terms of crank angles.
Question H

(h) What are the limitations of liquid cooling system?

While more effective at maintaining uniform engine temperatures compared to air cooling, liquid cooling systems suffer from several distinct drawbacks:

Added Weight High Maintenance Parasitic Power Loss
  • Complexity and Weight: The system requires a radiator, water pump, thermostat, hoses, and coolant jackets, adding significant bulk and weight to the engine.
  • Leakage Risks: Vulnerable to coolant leaks from failed hoses, radiator punctures, or blown head gaskets, which can instantly lead to catastrophic engine overheating.
  • Freezing/Boiling: Requires a specific mix of anti-freeze to prevent coolant from freezing in cold climates or boiling over in extreme heat.
  • Parasitic Loss: Driving the mechanical water pump and cooling fan consumes a portion of the engine's power output.
Question I

(i) What is crankcase blowby? How it is controlled?

Crankcase blowby is the leakage of unburned air-fuel mixture and high-pressure combustion gases from the cylinder, past the piston rings, and into the crankcase. If left uncontrolled, blowby pressurizes the crankcase, degrades the engine oil, and releases severe hydrocarbon emissions.

✓ Control Mechanism: PCV System

Blowby is controlled using a Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV) system. A PCV valve uses intake manifold vacuum to draw the blowby gases out of the crankcase and route them back into the intake manifold, where they are re-burned in the combustion chamber.

Question J

(j) Explain the sensors used for temperature measurements.

Modern IC engines rely heavily on temperature measurements for the ECU to adjust fuel injection and ignition timing. The two most common types of sensors used are:

SENSOR 01 ★

Thermistors (NTC type)

These are resistors whose electrical resistance drops significantly as temperature rises (Negative Temperature Coefficient). They are widely used for monitoring Engine Coolant Temperature (ECT) and Intake Air Temperature (IAT) due to their high sensitivity at moderate temperatures.

SENSOR 02

Thermocouples

Made of two dissimilar metals joined at a junction, generating a voltage proportional to the temperature difference. They are extremely robust and are exclusively used for measuring extreme heat, such as Exhaust Gas Temperature (EGT).
