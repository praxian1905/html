Question A

(a) What are the assumptions made in air standard cycle analysis?

✓ Solution Framework

To simplify the complex processes of an actual engine cycle, the following thermodynamic assumptions are made:

Ideal Gas Law Constant Specific Heats
  • The working fluid is a perfect gas (air) and obeys the ideal gas equation of state ($PV = mRT$) throughout the cycle.
  • The specific heats ($C_p$ and $C_v$) of the air remain constant at all temperatures.
  • All compression and expansion processes are assumed to be reversible and adiabatic (isentropic), meaning no heat loss and no internal friction.
  • The process operates on a closed cycle, where heat is added from an external source and rejected to an external sink, rather than internal combustion.
Question B

(b) Why a rich mixture is required for maximum power?

⚡ Key Insight

A rich mixture guarantees that all available oxygen in the cylinder is fully utilized during combustion.

For an engine to produce maximum power, the maximum amount of heat energy must be released per cycle. By providing a rich mixture (excess fuel), the engine ensures that every molecule of oxygen inducted into the cylinder reacts with fuel.

Additionally, rich mixtures burn at a higher flame velocity, which causes the peak cylinder pressure to occur closer to Top Dead Center (TDC), maximizing the indicated power output.

Question C

(c) What are the factors which affect the process of carburetion?

Carburetion is highly dependent on both the physical properties of the fuel and the operating conditions of the engine. The primary factors include:

FACTOR 01

Engine Speed & Load

Determines the velocity of air passing through the venturi, affecting the pressure drop and fuel induction rate.

FACTOR 02 ★

Fuel Properties

Volatility, surface tension, and viscosity critically determine how well the fuel atomizes and vaporizes.

FACTOR 03

Air Temperature & Pressure

Higher intake temperatures improve fuel vaporization but reduce air density, affecting the air-fuel ratio.

FACTOR 04

Carburetor Design

The specific geometries of the venturi throat, main nozzle size, and idle circuits.

Question D

(d) What is ignition lag?

In Spark Ignition (SI) engines, ignition lag (or ignition delay) is the time interval between the exact moment the spark discharges across the spark plug gap and the actual onset of combustion.

Ignition Lag = Time(Onset of Combustion) - Time(Spark Discharge) Usually measured in milliseconds or degrees of crank angle.

During this period, a pre-flame reaction occurs where the mixture absorbs heat and creates the initial flame nucleus before a measurable pressure rise is detected in the cylinder.

Question E

(e) What is meant by delay period?

In Compression Ignition (CI) engines, the delay period refers to the time interval between the start of fuel injection from the nozzle and the initiation of auto-ignition.

✓ Two Phases of Delay

The delay period is divided into the physical delay (time taken for atomization, vaporization, and mixing) and the chemical delay (time required for pre-flame chemical reactions to reach auto-ignition temperature).

Question F

(f) Why is over-cooling in an engine harmful?

While cooling is necessary to prevent thermal damage, over-cooling introduces several detrimental effects to engine efficiency and longevity.

Thermal Efficiency Drop Increased Viscosity Acid Condensation
  • Reduced Thermal Efficiency: Excessive cooling removes too much heat from the combustion chamber, heat that could have otherwise been converted into useful work.
  • Increased Friction: Over-cooled cylinder walls lower the temperature of the lubricating oil, increasing its viscosity and thereby increasing the mechanical friction of the piston.
  • Corrosive Wear: Low operating temperatures can cause moisture and combustion by-products (like sulfuric acid) to condense on the cylinder walls, leading to accelerated wear.
Question G

(g) What are the various methods of measuring indicated power?

Indicated Power (the total power developed by combustion inside the cylinder) can be measured or estimated using the following primary methods:

METHOD 01

Indicator Diagram Method

Using an engine indicator to plot a P-V diagram to find the mean effective pressure (IMEP).

METHOD 02

Morse Test

Used for multi-cylinder engines by cutting off cylinders one by one to find individual indicated powers.

METHOD 03

Willan's Line Method

Extrapolating the fuel consumption vs. brake power curve to zero fuel consumption (primarily for CI engines).

METHOD 04

Motoring Test

Driving the engine via an external motor without firing to measure friction power, adding it to brake power.

Question H

(h) What are the main constituents of exhaust emission from petrol engines?

The emissions from an SI (petrol) engine consist of both harmless combustion products and harmful pollutants.

Emission Category Constituents Primary Cause
Primary Pollutants Carbon Monoxide (CO) Incomplete combustion, particularly in rich mixtures.
Primary Pollutants Unburned Hydrocarbons (HC) Quenching at cylinder walls and incomplete burning.
Primary Pollutants Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) High peak combustion temperatures causing atmospheric $N_2$ to react with $O_2$.
Harmless / Greenhouse $CO_2$, $H_2O$, $N_2$ Complete stoichiometric combustion of hydrocarbon chains.
Question I

(i) What do you understand by blending of fuels?

Fuel blending is the process of mixing two or more distinct types of fuels or fuel grades to achieve specific, optimized physical and chemical properties that a single base fuel cannot provide.

⚡ Common Objectives

Blending is typically done to increase the Octane Rating (preventing knock in SI engines), adjust volatility for easier cold starting, or incorporate renewable sources (e.g., blending 10% ethanol with petrol to create E10, which reduces harmful emissions).

Question J

(j) State the advantages of closed cycle gas turbine plant.

Unlike open-cycle plants, a closed-cycle gas turbine recycles its working fluid, utilizing an external heat exchanger for combustion heat input.

No Internal Fouling High Part-Load Efficiency Fuel Flexibility
  • No Turbine Fouling: The combustion products do not pass through the turbine blades, eliminating corrosion, erosion, and fouling from ash or particulate matter.
  • Fuel Flexibility: Because combustion is external, cheaper or lower-grade fuels (like coal, peat, or even nuclear heat) can be used.
  • Higher Density Fluid: The system can operate at higher base pressures, reducing the specific volume of the working fluid and significantly reducing the physical size of the plant components for a given output.
  • Better Part-Load Efficiency: Power output can be varied by changing the absolute pressure of the working fluid while keeping temperatures and speeds constant, maintaining high efficiency at part loads.
