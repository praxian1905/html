Question A

(a) Describe the Carnot Cycle.

The Carnot Cycle is a theoretical, highly idealized thermodynamic cycle that establishes the absolute maximum possible thermal efficiency operating between two fixed temperature limits ($T_{max}$ and $T_{min}$).

✓ 4 Reversible Steps

The cycle consists of four distinct, totally reversible processes:

PROCESS 1-2

Reversible Isothermal Expansion

Heat is added from a high-temperature source at constant $T_{max}$.

PROCESS 2-3

Reversible Adiabatic Expansion

Working fluid expands isentropically, lowering temperature to $T_{min}$.

PROCESS 3-4

Reversible Isothermal Compression

Heat is rejected to a cold sink at constant $T_{min}$.

PROCESS 4-1

Reversible Adiabatic Compression

Fluid is compressed isentropically, restoring temperature to $T_{max}$.

Question B

(b) Why do we need a Flywheel in the IC Engine?

In reciprocating internal combustion engines, the generation of torque is non-uniform across the cycles. A flywheel serves as a crucial mechanical energy reservoir to balance this variation.

⚡ Kinetic Storage Engine

During the single power stroke, the engine produces excess work. The flywheel stores this excess energy as rotational kinetic energy. During the remaining three non-power strokes (intake, compression, exhaust), the flywheel releases this stored energy to maintain crankshaft rotation.

This cyclical energy storage significantly reduces the coefficient of fluctuation of speed, ensuring smooth, uniform engine operation and preventing structural vibration.

Question C

(c) Differentiate between EURO 1 and EURO 2.

EURO 1 and EURO 2 are successive tiers of mandatory European vehicular emission standards implemented to minimize environmental pollution.

Feature EURO 1 (Implemented ~1992) EURO 2 (Implemented ~1996)
Catalytic Converters Made catalytic converters mandatory for all petrol cars to control tailpipe emissions. Optimized existing catalytic configurations to be much cleaner from startup.
CO Emission Limits Permitted up to 2.72 g/km of Carbon Monoxide for passenger cars. Drastically cut limits down to 2.2 g/km for petrol engines and 1.0 g/km for diesel.
HC + NOx combined Allowed unburned hydrocarbon and nitrogen oxide combined up to 0.97 g/km. Reduced thresholds strictly down to 0.5 g/km for petrol systems.
Diesel Particulates Introduced the first limits on Particulate Matter (PM). Tighter, lower PM constraints specifically targeting heavy city buses and cars.
Question D

(d) What are the IC engine performance measures?

Engine performance metrics represent parameters utilized to quantify and compare the operating capabilities, efficiency, and fuel conversion characteristics of an engine:

METRIC 01

Power Output

Indicated Power (IP) produced inside the chamber, and usable Brake Power (BP) delivered at the crankshaft.

METRIC 02 ★

Efficiencies

Thermal Efficiencies ($\eta_{ith}$, $\eta_{bth}$) indicating energy conversion, and Volumetric Efficiency ($\eta_v$) assessing breathing capability.

METRIC 03

Fuel Consumption

Specific Fuel Consumption (SFC), defining the mass of fuel consumed per unit of power produced per hour ($g/kWh$).

METRIC 04

Mean Effective Pressure

Theoretical parameter mapping the relative torque and load output independent of physical cylinder displacement constraints.

Question E

(e) What is an Idle stroke?

An idle stroke (commonly classified as a non-power stroke) refers to any phase of the engine operating cycle where no chemical combustion energy is being transformed into positive mechanical output work.

⚠️ Energy Absorption Phase

In a standard four-stroke engine cycle, the Intake, Compression, and Exhaust strokes are all idle strokes. Instead of producing work, they actively absorb small amounts of kinetic energy from the moving flywheel to successfully manage gas exchange and preparation.

Question F

(f) Discuss MPFI system in IC Engine.

MPFI (Multi-Point Fuel Injection) is an electronic induction system utilized in modern petrol engines where every single cylinder is assigned its own dedicated fuel injector.

Uniform Distribution Precise Metering Lower Emissions
  • Port Injection Location: Injectors are located in the intake manifold runners pointing directly toward each individual intake valve.
  • Electronic Metre Control: An Engine Control Unit (ECU) monitors air flow, speed, and temperature parameters to cycle the injector openings down to microsecond intervals.
  • Aesthetic Edge: By eliminating single-point carburetors, it solves non-uniform mixture variations among cylinders, optimizing specific power and dramatically cutting unburned hydrocarbon exhaust profile structures.
Question G

(g) What is the compression ratio in IC engine?

The compression ratio ($r$) is a primary geometric parameter of internal combustion engines that measures the degree to which the air-fuel mixture or air charge is compressed inside the cylinder during the compression stroke.

r = (Swept Volume + Clearance Volume) / Clearance Volume r = (V_s + V_c) / V_c Where: V_s = Swept volume (displaced by piston from BDC to TDC) V_c = Clearance volume (minimum volume remaining above TDC)

A higher compression ratio increases the theoretical thermal efficiency of the cycle, though it is limited by fuel knock thresholds in SI variants.

Question H

(h) Discuss the basic need for the ignition system.

In Spark Ignition (SI) engines, the air-fuel mixture inside the cylinder cannot auto-ignite under compression pressures alone. An external input is fundamentally required to start combustion.

✓ Functional Mandate

The system transforms low vehicle battery voltage ($12V$) into high electrical potentials ($20,000V–30,000V$) capable of jumping the spark plug gap. This high-energy electrical arc provides the localized activation energy necessary to initialize a self-sustaining flame nucleus at the precise calculated crank angle moment relative to engine load and velocity.

Question I

(i) What is an Octane Number?

The Octane Number is a standard chemical metric evaluating the anti-knock capability and auto-ignition resistance of a fuel when subjected to the temperatures and pressures of a Spark Ignition (SI) engine combustion chamber.

⚡ Standard Reference Scale

It is based on a reference percentage blend where highly knock-resistant Iso-octane is rated 100, and highly knock-prone n-heptane is rated 0. A fuel with an octane rating of 95 possesses the identical auto-ignition stability as a mixture of 95% iso-octane and 5% n-heptane.

Question J

(j) What is a Cetane Number?

The Cetane Number is a standard index measuring the chemical ignition readiness and delay characteristics of a fuel designed for Compression Ignition (CI / diesel) engines.

It explicitly evaluates the physical and chemical speed with which a fuel auto-ignites after injection into the compressed air chamber:

Cetane Value Profile Ignition Delay Characteristic Resulting Combustion Profile
High Cetane Rating Extremely short ignition delay period. Smoother pressure rise, easier cold starting, reduced engine knocking sound.
Low Cetane Rating Prolonged, long delay period. Excessive fuel accumulates before ignition, leading to sudden high-pressure shocks (diesel knock).
