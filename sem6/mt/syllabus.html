💡 Key Distinction

A jig guides the cutting tool (e.g. a drill bush guides the drill), while a fixture only locates and clamps the workpiece — the tool is guided by the machine itself.

02. Powder Metallurgy & Plastic Manufacturing

🧪 Unit Overview

Introduces two material-based manufacturing routes — powder metallurgy for metals and ceramics, and polymer processing via moulding and extrusion — with emphasis on process steps, parameters, and industrial applicability.

Powder Metallurgy

STEP 01

Powder Production

Atomisation, reduction, electrolysis, mechanical comminution.

STEP 02

Mixing & Blending

Combining powders with lubricants and binders for uniformity.

STEP 03

Compacting

Die compaction under high pressure to form the green compact.

STEP 04

Sintering

Heating below melting point — diffusion bonds particles together.

STEP 05

Finishing

Sizing, machining, impregnation, plating as required.

  • Steps of powder metallurgy — detailed process chain
  • Compacting — cold and hot isostatic pressing
  • Sintering — solid-state, liquid-phase sintering mechanisms
  • Applications of powder metallurgy
  • Self-lubricating bearings, cemented carbide tools
  • Refractory metal parts (W, Mo filaments)
  • Filters and porous components

Plastics Manufacturing

  • Types of plastics — thermoplastics vs. thermosets
  • Injection Moulding
  • Process sequence: plasticising → injection → cooling → ejection
  • Applications: complex near-net-shape components
  • Merits: high production rate, excellent surface finish, automation
  • Demerits: high tooling cost, limited to thermoplastics
  • Plastic Extrusion
  • Screw extruder design and operation
  • Products: pipes, sheets, films, profiles

Unit 05

05. Non-conventional Machining — Thermal Processes

⚡ Unit Overview

Studies thermal energy-based material removal — where heat rather than mechanical force drives cutting. Covers EDM in depth (including Wire-cut EDM), then Plasma Arc and Laser Beam Machining, concluding with Electron Beam technology.

Electric Discharge Machining (EDM)

  • Construction detail — machine structure, servo head, dielectric tank
  • Principle — controlled spark erosion between tool and workpiece in dielectric
  • Process parameters
  • Discharge voltage and current
  • Pulse on-time (Tₒₙ) and off-time (Tₒff)
  • Duty cycle and frequency
  • Material Removal Rate (MRR) — relationship with discharge energy
  • Surface finish — Ra as function of pulse energy; recast layer
  • Tool wear — electrode wear ratio; copper, graphite electrode materials
  • Dielectric fluid — kerosene, EDM oil, deionised water; flushing methods
  • Power and control circuits — RC circuit, transistorised pulse generator
  • Wire-cut EDM (WEDM)
  • Brass / stratified wire electrode (0.05–0.3 mm)
  • CNC contouring of complex 2D/3D profiles
  • No cutting force — ideal for hard, fragile materials
EDM Material Removal Rate: MRR ∝ I × Tₒₙ^0.4 / (Vm × ρ × Cp × Tm) where I = discharge current (A) Tₒₙ = pulse on-time (µs) Vm = melt volume fraction ρ = density of workpiece Cp = specific heat Tm = melting point

Plasma Arc Machining (PAM)

  • Generation of plasma — ionised gas at 8 000–25 000 °C
  • Transferred and non-transferred arc configurations
  • Applications — cutting stainless steel, aluminium, titanium alloys
  • Limitations — HAZ, surface oxidation, noise, radiation

Laser Beam Machining (LBM)

  • Laser types — Nd:YAG, CO₂, fibre laser
  • Principle — photon energy → heat → melting/vaporisation
  • Process parameters — power density, pulse duration, assist gas
  • Applications — drilling, cutting, micro-machining, marking
  • Merits — no tool wear, no contact, high precision
  • Limitations — HAZ, reflectivity of metals, high capital cost

Electron Beam Machining (EBM)

  • Principle — focused high-velocity electron beam converts KE → heat
  • Vacuum environment requirement (10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁶ torr)
  • Applications — micro-drilling, welding, lithography
  • Limitations — vacuum chamber size, X-ray shielding, cost
High Heat Input Recast Layer (EDM) Wire-Cut EDM Laser Precision No Cutting Force Hard Material Capability
Unit 06

06. Non-conventional Machining — Mechanical & Electrochemical

🌊 Unit Overview

Covers material removal by mechanical abrasion (AJM, WJM, AWJM, USM) and by electrochemical and chemical dissolution (ECM, CHM) — processes that produce no thermal damage and are suited to complex, hard, or delicate workpieces.

Mechanical Abrasive Processes

Process 01

Abrasive Jet Machining (AJM)

  • High-velocity abrasive + gas stream
  • Nozzle: WC, sapphire (0.18–0.4 mm)
  • Deburring, cleaning, frosting glass
Process 02

Water Jet Machining (WJM)

  • Pure water at 200–400 MPa
  • No abrasive — soft materials only
  • Food, foam, rubber, textiles
Process 03 ★

Abrasive Water Jet (AWJM)

  • Water + garnet/SiC abrasive
  • Cuts metals, composites, stone
  • No HAZ — cold cutting process
Process 04

Ultrasonic Machining (USM)

  • Tool vibrates at 20–40 kHz
  • Abrasive slurry impact + cavitation
  • Ceramics, glass, hard brittle materials

Electrochemical Machining (ECM)

  • Principle — anodic dissolution of workpiece (Faraday's law); tool is cathode
  • Elements of ECM
  • Power supply — low voltage (5–30 V), high current (50–40 000 A)
  • Tool (cathode) — copper, brass, stainless steel
  • Electrolyte — NaCl, NaNO₃ solution; flushed at high velocity
  • Inter-electrode gap — 0.1–0.6 mm controlled by servo feed
  • Advantages — no tool wear, no thermal damage, complex shapes in one pass
  • Applications — turbine blade profiling, gun rifling, die sinking
  • Limitations — electrolyte disposal, poor accuracy for sharp corners, high power
ECM Material Removal Rate (Faraday's Law): MRR = (I × A) / (F × z × ρ) where I = current (A) A = atomic weight of workpiece material F = Faraday's constant (96 485 C/mol) z = valency of dissolution ρ = density of workpiece (g/cm³)

Chemical Machining (CHM)

  • Principle — controlled chemical dissolution using etchant; no current required
  • Etchants and Maskants
  • Etchants: FeCl₃ (for Cu), HF/HNO₃ (for Al), NaOH (for Al)
  • Maskants: elastomers, photoresists, pressure-sensitive tapes
  • Photochemical machining (PCM) uses photolithography for masking
  • Advantages — simultaneous machining of complex contours, thin sheets, no burr
  • Applications — PCBs, aerospace skin panels, decorative etching
  • Limitations — undercutting, slow MRR, hazardous chemical handling
